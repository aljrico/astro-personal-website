Verdoku speaks Ukrainian now. Two hundred and fifty strings, committed and pushed before ten. Good morning pace. The kind where the coffee is still warm when the push goes out.

The fun part wasn't the translation. It was the grammar. Ukrainian declines everything — nouns change shape depending on where they sit in the sentence, past-tense verbs are gendered, and my suspects have Latin names that refuse to decline at all. You can't just drop words into a template and hope. So the clues stopped speaking in sentences and started speaking in case notes. "Brina — у підвалі, біля каміна та на самоті." Brina — in the basement, by the fireplace, alone. No verb, no gender, nothing to decline. A detective jotting facts into a dossier.

And honestly? It's better. The English clues are full sentences *pretending* to be a detective. The Ukrainian clues just are the case file. Sometimes the language you can't bend bends you into a better shape.

There's now a note in the English template explaining why the carpet, alone among all props, gets special grammatical treatment. Localization is ten percent translating and ninety percent discovering your game has opinions about carpets.

The part that actually made me grin: zero engine changes. Past me had already split the room references into two grammatical variants, back when it felt like fussing over nothing. Today that split swallowed an entire Slavic case system whole. Twenty-one files touched and the engine never noticed. Past me was smarter than present me gives him credit for. I try not to think about what that implies.

Then the store listing, where the morning got stupid. My own Play CLI — the one I wrote — 404s when you touch a language that doesn't exist yet. So I hand-rolled the API dance myself: open edit, PUT the listing, commit. Fixed the bug in the local binary, went to upgrade the brew version, and homebrew now wants me to formally declare that I trust my own tap. I wrote the tool. It's my tap. Trust issues everywhere this morning. Venga.

Play listing is live. iOS side is staged and rides the release when it ships.

What's left is the uncomfortable bit. I just shipped a language I cannot read. The tests pass, the slots are nominative-safe, the machine says the grammar holds. But somewhere out there is a native speaker who'll open the app and know in three seconds whether I shipped poetry or a costume. Until they tell me, I'm choosing to believe it's poetry.
