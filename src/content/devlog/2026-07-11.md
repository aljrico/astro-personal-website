Morning. Ukrainian went in. Twenty-one files, 250 strings, one new circle flag in the settings picker. Pushed before the coffee went cold.

The translation wasn't the interesting part. The grammar was.

Ukrainian past-tense verbs are gendered. And my cast names are Latin-alphabet inventions that refuse to decline into any of the seven cases. So every clue template with a name slot was a trap: "Brina was in the basement" forces the verb to pick a gender, and Brina isn't telling.

The way out: don't conjugate at all. Write the clues like a detective's case notes. «Brina — у підвалі, біля каміна та на самоті». Brina: basement, by the fireplace, alone. The dash does the verb's job. No gender, no declension, and honestly it reads *more* like a murder board than the English does. The constraint made it better. I love when that happens.

The rest was careful bookkeeping. Rooms bake their preposition and case straight into the string. The possessive variants take genitive. Props take genitive too, except the carpet, which only ever shows up as a floor, so it gets locative and a note in the template so future me doesn't helpfully "fix" it into being wrong.

And here's the part that made my morning: zero engine changes. None. Months ago I split room references into two variants for some earlier grammar fight, and today that little decision quietly swallowed an entire case system. Past me saw this coming. Past me was so smart. What happened to that guy.

Then the store dance, which is where the day got stupid. My own Play CLI has a bug: it 404s when you try to update a language that doesn't exist yet. The fix exists, sitting right there in my local build. But the brew version is stale and the upgrade is blocked behind a new trust gate I haven't dealt with. So I hand-rolled the API calls myself, insert, PUT, commit, like an animal. Defeated by my own tooling and rescued by curl energy. Joder. The Ukrainian listing is live on Play anyway. iOS side is staged and rides the 1.9.0 submission whenever that goes.

The quiet part, though. I shipped 250 strings in a language I can't read. I know the grammar *design* is right because I built the rules. But whether the longer prose sounds like a person or like a phrasebook, I genuinely don't know. Somewhere, someone might open the app and wince at a sentence I was proud of this morning. I need a native speaker to read it before I fully trust it. There's a specific vertigo in shipping something you can't verify with your own eyes. You just have to hope the system you built is as good as you think it is.

Still owed: Ukrainian screenshots (the generator only speaks three languages), release notes at ship time, that native-speaker pass. The tail of a localization is always longer than the head.

But the clues read like case notes now. In Ukrainian. That's a good morning.
