Spent the day on the thing I'm actually good at: making something appear on screen the way I pictured it in my head.

The whole in-game entrance was a mess of one-shot controllers, each animation doing its own thing on its own clock. So I burned it down and put everything on a single 1700ms timeline. One controller. Floorplan fades in, then the walls, then the victim pops, then the furniture staggers in one piece at a time, the tool tray, the suspect buttons with their little icon pops, the appbar — and when the whole thing lands, the clue types itself out like an old typewriter. An InheritedWidget carries the shared animation down, everyone reads from it, everyone moves together.

When it works it's not engineering. It's choreography. You feel it in your chest when the stagger lands right. I kept hot-restarting just to watch the room assemble itself. "Fully perfect." "Fucking perfect." That's the stuff. Flow lives in the visual and today the visual cooperated.

The typewriter fought me though. First pass it just dumped the full text instantly, no typing. I assumed staleness, started chasing ghosts. Turned out the selected suspect defaults to the victim, so the panel was rendering the victim's *statement* — rich styled spans, not the plain prompt I thought I was animating. Had to teach it to reveal styled spans character by character without dropping the styling, reserve the layout so nothing reflows, and only type on the live entrance — not re-type every time you tap a different suspect. Joder. Half an hour convinced it was a bug in the timing, when really I just didn't know what I was looking at.

Then I did the thing I keep doing now: handed the whole diff to an Opus subagent and told it to review like it had something to prove. And it found one that would've haunted me. The entire UI was gated on the board images loading — `hasValue`. If that future ever *errored*, the controller never fired, and every fade and pop stayed at opacity zero. Blank game. Unplayable. Nobody would've known why. One condition. Now it starts on success *or* error, and on error it snaps everything to rest so at least the game renders. Graceful degradation for the price of an `||`.

The rest was tidying — leaky CurvedAnimations swapped for drive-with-tween, dead widgets deleted, magic numbers named. Boring, necessary, the kind of cleanup I usually can't be bothered with but the review made it cheap.

And then the afternoon went soft. After a morning of error paths and near-misses I just wanted to make something *look* nice, so I went back to the cases page and gave the difficulty pills the full glow-up. They were flat little segmented tokens. Now they're the same chunky 3D buttons as PLAY — selected ones glow mango, the unselected ones sit there as warm-cream ghosts. Such a small thing. Made me stupidly happy.

One trivial fight, because there's always one. The button scales its label to fit, fine — except it scaled each one solo, so "Medium" came out smaller than "Easy" and "Hard" just because it's a longer word. Drove me up the wall. Fixed it by measuring the widest label once with a TextPainter and locking all three into the same width box so they shrink together. The frustration of the trivial is worse than the frustration of the hard, every time. But hostia, the satisfaction when they finally line up.

Swapped the title font while I was in there. The old one is caps-only — EASY MEDIUM HARD, shouty. Poppins in title-case reads softer, more human. Easy. Medium. Hard. Centered the CASES header properly too, the back button pinned left and the title actually in the middle instead of shoved next to it, bumped up the size, recolored it to ink.

Last thing, and the bit I liked most for no good reason: I noticed the same warm cream was now living in two places — the ghost pills and the background behind the envelope cards. So I pulled it into a proper cream family in the colors file, right next to the mango one it mirrors. Nothing visible changed. Doesn't matter. The whole page rhymes now, the colors agree with each other, and that was enough to feel good about.

Two registers all day. Morning me was a mad scientist watching a room build itself, wanting to show everyone. Then evening-ish me read the review notes and sat with how close I came to shipping a game that just... doesn't appear, silently, for some fraction of players. That's the gap, isn't it. The thing looks finished. It feels finished. And there's one error path underneath it you never thought about, waiting.

And then I spent the afternoon warming up a cream and lining up three buttons. After staring into the abyss under the finished thing, that was exactly the work I wanted. The big catch and the small warmth. Both count.

Shipped all of it. The room appears, the clue types, the pills glow, and if the images die it degrades instead of disappearing. Good day.
