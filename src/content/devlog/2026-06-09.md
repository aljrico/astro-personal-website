Spent the morning on the thing I'm actually good at: making something appear on screen the way I pictured it in my head.

The whole in-game entrance was a mess of one-shot controllers, each animation doing its own thing on its own clock. So I burned it down and put everything on a single 1700ms timeline. One controller. Floorplan fades in, then the walls, then the victim pops, then the furniture staggers in one piece at a time, the tool tray, the suspect buttons with their little icon pops, the appbar — and when the whole thing lands, the clue types itself out like an old typewriter. An InheritedWidget carries the shared animation down, everyone reads from it, everyone moves together.

When it works it's not engineering. It's choreography. You feel it in your chest when the stagger lands right. I kept hot-restarting just to watch the room assemble itself. "Fully perfect." "Fucking perfect." That's the stuff. Flow lives in the visual and today the visual cooperated.

The typewriter fought me though. First pass it just dumped the full text instantly, no typing. I assumed staleness, started chasing ghosts. Turned out the selected suspect defaults to the victim, so the panel was rendering the victim's *statement* — rich styled spans, not the plain prompt I thought I was animating. Had to teach it to reveal styled spans character by character without dropping the styling, reserve the layout so nothing reflows, and only type on the live entrance — not re-type every time you tap a different suspect. Joder. Half an hour convinced it was a bug in the timing, when really I just didn't know what I was looking at.

Then I did the thing I keep doing now: handed the whole diff to an Opus subagent and told it to review like it had something to prove. And it found one that would've haunted me. The entire UI was gated on the board images loading — `hasValue`. If that future ever *errored*, the controller never fired, and every fade and pop stayed at opacity zero. Blank game. Unplayable. Nobody would've known why. One condition. Now it starts on success *or* error, and on error it snaps everything to rest so at least the game renders. Graceful degradation for the price of an `||`.

The rest was tidying — leaky CurvedAnimations swapped for drive-with-tween, dead widgets deleted, magic numbers named. Boring, necessary, the kind of cleanup I usually can't be bothered with but the review made it cheap.

Then I just kept going. The visual was cooperating and you don't waste a day like that on architecture, so I moved over to the cases page.

The difficulty selector was a row of flat little tokens. Made them real. Same chunky 3D button as PLAY — mango when it's the one you've picked, a warm-cream ghost when it's not. Suddenly they look like buttons you'd actually want to press instead of labels pretending to be choices.

One stupid snag. Each pill was scaling its own text to fit, so "Medium" — the longest word — came out smallest. Wrong in the way your eye catches before your brain does. The fix is the satisfying kind: measure the widest label once, force all three into the same box, let them scale as a set. Trivial. But the trivial stuff is the stuff that either looks right or doesn't, and there's nowhere to hide from it.

Also learned the title font only speaks in capitals. EASY, MEDIUM, HARD, all shouting. Wanted it quieter and more legible, so Easy/Medium/Hard in Poppins instead. Small thing. Reads better. While I was in there I fixed the CASES header too — it was shoved left, the back arrow and the title fighting over the same row. Full-width now, arrow pinned to the edge, title dead center, bigger, inked darker. It just sits right. Came back to it a bit later and swapped the bare back arrow for a proper SoftButton — the same frosted-white rounded tile as the settings gear on the home screen. Little consistency win. The app's chrome is starting to speak one language.

Then the envelope card had this greyish cream behind the dark "?" card that didn't match the new ghost pills, so I warmed it up. Then noticed the same cream was now hardcoded in two files. Pulled it into a proper cream family next to the mango one — cream, creamShade, creamStrong. Pure refactor, nothing moved on screen — the kind of thing I'd normally call procrastination in a lab coat. But a color living in two places is the one cleanup that's cheaper to do than to ignore. Two minutes. Done.

Then a clue that was lying to people. One of the deduction lines reads "X was with the only person in the Cellar" — except X *is* the person it's describing, so it quietly refers to itself. Confusing in the way that makes you read it three times. The easy fix was to weaken the logic underneath — "only" becomes "at least one" — and the sentence would've sorted itself out. I didn't. That would've made the clue nearly redundant with another one, risked puzzles that need an outright guess, and knocked the whole difficulty calibration out of true, which means regenerating everything. All that to fix a sentence. No. The puzzle is fine; the wording is the problem. So I fixed only the wording — special-cased that one clue shape to say "was in the Cellar with just one other person" — and left every byte of the actual logic exactly where it was. Even wrote a test for it, the first one I've got that checks what a clue actually *says* to a player. Difficulty: identical. Only the words moved.

And that's where the day turned into a different day entirely.

I want harder puzzles. Not bigger boards, not more suspects — the same little 5×5 room, just harder to crack. The question is how, and the answer was hiding in the generator the whole time. When it builds a puzzle it checks two things: exactly one solution, *and* that a human could reach it by pure deduction, no guessing. If its guess-free solver gets stuck, it bins the puzzle — even when the puzzle is perfectly, uniquely solvable. The solver just isn't smart enough to prove it. So you don't need bigger boards. You need a smarter solver. Teach it more ways to reason and it stops throwing the hard ones away.

Normally this is where mad-scientist me starts coding techniques at random. Instead I did the thing I pretend I don't do: I measured first. Built a read-only harness that watches the generator discard puzzles and asks, for each one, which single reasoning technique would've cracked it. Ran it over thousands of seeds.

Hostia. Ninety-six percent. The generator is binning ninety-six percent of its unique puzzles as "too hard for me to prove" — twenty-seven times more than it keeps. There's a mountain of good, hard, genuinely solvable puzzles going in the trash every single run and I never knew it was there. And the techniques that would save them clustered cleanly: projected line singles crack 42%, hidden singles 34%, a line-pairs technique 10%. One candidate I was sure I'd need turned out to crack basically nothing — dropped it before writing a line. Three techniques, ~87% of the pile. Decisive go.

So I built them. One tier at a time, cheapest reasoning first, and every one of them *sound* — because sound is the entire game here. A solver that "proves" a wrong answer is worse than a dumb one; it'd ship broken, unsolvable puzzles dressed up as fair ones. So each technique only fires when it can actually justify itself, with occupancy guards and a debug invariant screaming if any step ever drops a cell that belongs to the true solution. I harvested the generator's own rejected puzzles as test fixtures — solve the real ones it gave up on, not toy cases. Projected singles alone: 30% of the pile cracked outright. Add hidden singles: 54%. Add the pairs: 58%. The pairs only bought four points, so I stopped there. Knowing when to quit — for me, the rare skill.

I sent the plan to a tougher reviewer between phases and it earned its keep: caught one technique that could've silently no-op'd its way into unsoundness, and pushed me to add a mid-run invariant I'd have skipped out of laziness. The whole thing stays off by default, so every puzzle the game ships *today* is byte-identical to yesterday's. Nothing changed. That's the point, and also the punchline.

Because here's where I left it. The engine can now certify a whole harder class of puzzle — sound, tested, reviewed — and not one player will feel a single harder puzzle yet. The last finding of the day was that "difficulty" in this game has always just meant board size: Easy is 5×5, Hard is 7×7. These new techniques are a completely separate axis — harder *thinking* on the same board. Wiring that into the actual curve, the thing a person would touch, is tomorrow. I built the machine. I haven't turned the key.

Two people built this app today. Morning me was an artist — choreographing a room into existence, hot-restarting just to watch it land, doing the thing I'll happily tell you I'm good at. Afternoon me was the physicist I keep insisting I'm not: phases, a harness before a single feature, percentages, knowing the exact point the curve flattened and stopping on it. And the stupid thing is the physicist had the better day. The clean, measure-twice version of me shipped sound code for hours and barely broke a sweat.

Both halves were chasing the same ghost, though. This morning it was a game that looks finished and silently doesn't appear if one image fails to load. This afternoon it was a solver that looks correct and silently proves something false. Same fear, different floor of the building — the error path you don't see, sitting under the surface that looks done.

The visible stuff shipped this morning: the room appears, the clue types, the pills look like buttons, the cases header sits where it should, the page runs warmer. The afternoon shipped infrastructure nobody can see. Whether that was the best use of a day the visuals were finally cooperating, I genuinely don't know. But I know there are far more hard puzzles waiting in this game than I thought there were this morning. Mañana I turn the key. Good day.
