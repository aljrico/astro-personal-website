Spent the morning on the thing I'm actually good at: making something appear on screen the way I pictured it in my head.

The whole in-game entrance was a mess of one-shot controllers, each animation doing its own thing on its own clock. So I burned it down and put everything on a single 1700ms timeline. One controller. Floorplan fades in, then the walls, then the victim pops, then the furniture staggers in one piece at a time, the tool tray, the suspect buttons with their little icon pops, the appbar — and when the whole thing lands, the clue types itself out like an old typewriter. An InheritedWidget carries the shared animation down, everyone reads from it, everyone moves together.

When it works it's not engineering. It's choreography. You feel it in your chest when the stagger lands right. I kept hot-restarting just to watch the room assemble itself. "Fully perfect." "Fucking perfect." That's the stuff. Flow lives in the visual and today the visual cooperated.

The typewriter fought me though. First pass it just dumped the full text instantly, no typing. I assumed staleness, started chasing ghosts. Turned out the selected suspect defaults to the victim, so the panel was rendering the victim's *statement* — rich styled spans, not the plain prompt I thought I was animating. Had to teach it to reveal styled spans character by character without dropping the styling, reserve the layout so nothing reflows, and only type on the live entrance — not re-type every time you tap a different suspect. Joder. Half an hour convinced it was a bug in the timing, when really I just didn't know what I was looking at.

Then I did the thing I keep doing now: handed the whole diff to an Opus subagent and told it to review like it had something to prove. And it found one that would've haunted me. The entire UI was gated on the board images loading — `hasValue`. If that future ever *errored*, the controller never fired, and every fade and pop stayed at opacity zero. Blank game. Unplayable. Nobody would've known why. One condition. Now it starts on success *or* error, and on error it snaps everything to rest so at least the game renders. Graceful degradation for the price of an `||`.

The rest was tidying — leaky CurvedAnimations swapped for drive-with-tween, dead widgets deleted, magic numbers named. Boring, necessary, the kind of cleanup I usually can't be bothered with but the review made it cheap.

Then I just kept going. The visual was cooperating and you don't waste a day like that on architecture, so I moved over to the cases page.

The difficulty selector was a row of flat little tokens. Made them real. Same chunky 3D button as PLAY — mango when it's the one you've picked, a warm-cream ghost when it's not. Suddenly they look like buttons you'd actually want to press instead of labels pretending to be choices.

One stupid snag. Each pill was scaling its own text to fit, so "Medium" — the longest word — came out smallest. Wrong in the way your eye catches before your brain does. The fix is the satisfying kind: measure the widest label once, force all three into the same box, let them scale as a set. Trivial. But the trivial stuff is the stuff that either looks right or doesn't, and there's nowhere to hide from it.

Also learned the title font only speaks in capitals. EASY, MEDIUM, HARD, all shouting. Wanted it quieter and more legible, so Easy/Medium/Hard in Poppins instead. Small thing. Reads better. While I was in there I fixed the CASES header too — it was shoved left, the back arrow and the title fighting over the same row. Full-width now, arrow pinned to the edge, title dead center, bigger, inked darker. Later I went back and made the arrow itself a proper frosted soft-button tile, the same one as the settings gear on home, so it stops being a lonely icon and joins the family. It just sits right.

Then the envelope card — almost by accident. It had this greyish cream behind the dark "?" card that didn't match the new ghost pills, so I warmed it up. Then noticed the same cream was now hardcoded in two files. Pulled it into a proper cream family next to the mango one — cream, creamShade, creamStrong. Pure refactor, nothing moved on screen — the kind of thing I'd normally call procrastination in a lab coat. But a color living in two places is the one cleanup that's cheaper to do than to ignore. Two minutes. Done.

Around lunch a smaller thing turned into a quiet little principle. One clue type reads "{X} was with the only person in the kitchen" — except X *is* the person, so it points at itself and reads like a riddle nobody asked for. The lazy fix was to weaken the underlying logic: exactly-one becomes at-least-one and the self-reference goes away. I didn't. Weaken it and it's almost redundant with another clue, and worse, you start letting through puzzles that need a guess — which quietly poisons the difficulty calibration and forces me to regenerate everything. The complaint was about *words*, not logic. So I fixed the words and only the words: special-cased the one variant that clashes, left the engine untouched, "was in the Cellar with just one other person." Wrote the first app-side test for clue text while I was in there. Refuse to touch the difficulty machinery for a wording bug. Hold that thought.

Because then the day turned, and I spent the rest of it doing the exact opposite.

I've wanted the puzzles harder for a while. The obvious lever is bigger boards, more suspects — and I hate it. More size isn't more interesting, it's just more. The elegant lever is the solver. The generator only ships a puzzle if a guess-free solver can crack it by pure deduction. Which means: if the solver is dumb, every uniquely-solvable puzzle that happens to need reasoning the solver doesn't *know* gets thrown on the floor. Make the solver smarter and you don't change the boards at all — you unlock a backlog of harder-but-still-fair puzzles that were always there.

That's the bet. So before building anything, I measured. Phase zero, read-only, no logic touched — just instrument the generator, run it over thousands of seeds, and count what it's discarding and why. The number came back and it stopped me. Ninety-six percent. The generator is throwing away roughly twenty-seven good puzzles for every one it ships, purely because the solver gives up too early. Hostia. There's a whole game's worth of difficulty sitting in the reject pile, and I've been generating around it this whole time.

And the same pass told me *which* techniques would save them. Projected line singles, hidden line singles, Hall line pairs — three techniques crack about eighty-seven percent of the pile. Cell pairs, the thing I assumed I'd need, basically never matter. Drop them. A decisive go, and a shopping list, before I'd written a line of the actual solver. The physicist brain, used for once exactly the way it's supposed to be used: measure first so you don't spend three days building the wrong thing.

Then I built the three, cheapest-first, so the solver always finds the simplest path and the difficulty ratings stay honest. And the whole time the obsession was the same word: *sound*. A guess-free solver is only allowed to do things it can *prove* — it can never assume. So every technique is exclusion-based, no occupancy hand-waving, and where a shortcut would be unsound I built a guard for it and then wrote a synthetic puzzle whose only job is to try to make the solver lie, and confirmed the guard keeps it honest. Projected singles alone certified about thirty percent of the reject pile. Add hidden singles and it's over half. Add Hall pairs and it's fifty-eight.

And there Hall pairs only bought four points. So I stopped. Three techniques, no fourth. The triples generalization was already sitting there, one recursion away, and I left it. Diminishing returns are diminishing returns; the cost-benefit math doesn't care how elegant the next thing would've been.

I ran each phase past a second model the way I ran the morning UI past the Opus subagent — same instinct, different domain. Tell it to tear the diff apart, find the unsound step, prove me wrong. It found a real one (a count of zero that should've been a contradiction and was silently doing nothing) and it green-lit the rest. Two reviewers in one day, neither of them me, both load-bearing. I don't fully trust myself alone anymore and I've decided that's maturity, not weakness.

The discipline that I'm quietly proud of: none of this touches a single shipped puzzle yet. There's a default cap that keeps the structural techniques off, so every existing case is byte-for-byte what it was this morning. The new depth is opt-in until I wire the curve. And the last piece of the day was the reframe that makes the whole thing click — difficulty in this game has *always* secretly meant board size. Easy is 5×5, Medium is 6, Hard is 7. These technique tiers are a completely orthogonal axis. Depth, not size. Which is exactly the thing I said I wanted at the top of the afternoon. I can make a puzzle harder without making it bigger now. That's the whole point and I didn't fully see it until it was built.

So look at the two halves of this day. Morning me was a mad scientist watching a room build itself, hot-restarting just to feel the stagger land, working entirely on gut and chest. Afternoon me wrote soundness proofs and a counterexample test and ran a measurement study and shut a technique down on a four-percent margin and sent the diff out to be torn apart. The artist and the physicist, in the same day, and here's the uncomfortable honest part: I'm the guy who says programming is art and code quality is overrated and gut beats spreadsheets — and the afternoon was nothing but spreadsheets, and it was the best work of the day, and I loved it. Not as a cocoon. The measurement *was* the work. Twenty-seven puzzles thrown away for every one kept; you don't feel your way to that, you count it.

The engine's done. What's left is the part that touches players — extend the three ramps so Medium and Hard actually reach for the deeper tiers — and that's tomorrow's app-side work. Funny how the satisfaction's already draining now that the hard part's proven and the rest is wiring. The show must go on, I suppose.

But today the optimism held. The room appears, and degrades instead of disappearing if the images die. The difficulty pills look like buttons, the header sits where it should, the page is a little warmer. And somewhere underneath all of it, the solver got smart enough to stop throwing away the good stuff. Good day. A different kind than I thought I was having at 11am, but a good one.
