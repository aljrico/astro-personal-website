Built the entire streak system in one session. Model, service, provider, widget, database migration, the works. That sentence makes it sound clean. It wasn't. But the pieces fell into place faster than expected and that's the kind of afternoon that reminds you why you do this.

The streak card sits on the home screen now — dark gradient, senyera stripe across the top, seven-day rolling calendar with little Catalan day labels. Dl, Dt, Dc, Dj, Dv, Ds, Dg. There's a 2AM grace period baked into the service because nobody should lose a streak because they were coding until 1:47am. Ask me how I know.

The fun part was the constraint: minimum streak is always 1, never 0. You opened the app. You showed up. That counts. I like that philosophy more than I expected to.

Then the polish spiral started.

Centered today in the week row — three days back, today, three days forward. Duolingo does this and it works because your eye lands on the present. Filled the inactive day circles with a subtle white tint instead of leaving them hollow. Switched to BarlowSemiCondensed at 12px because the day labels were breathing too much.

Tried IEC lowercase labels. dl, dt, dc. Technically more correct. Looked like a whisper. Capitalized them back. Dl, Dt, Dc. Better.

Then I tried swapping the Lucide flame outline for Material's filled `local_fire_department` icon. Hostia, no. It looked like a safety warning on a gas appliance. Reverted in under a minute. Some experiments you run just to confirm your instincts were right.

Found a bug on the way out — the day labels are static, not rolling with actual weekdays. Thursday says Thursday regardless of what day the column actually represents. It's the kind of thing nobody would notice for a week and then it would be all they see. Noted for next time.

Pushed to main. TestFlight build spinning up. The streak card looks good. Not "I need to show everyone" good — more like "this belongs here" good. Quieter satisfaction. The kind where you close the laptop and don't immediately reopen it.

Except I did reopen it.

---

The topics screen had been bothering me. A flat scroll of heavy cards, every topic the same visual weight, no hierarchy. You open the app for Bombers and you're staring at a wall of rectangles. So I tore it up. Replaced the whole thing with collapsible accordion blocs — one supercard per bloc, topic rows nested inside.

The supercard has a letter badge, bloc name, a subtitle that counts temes and completats, a 40px donut chart with the percentage label, and a chevron that animates on expand. Inside: compact topic rows with 36px code badges (A1, B3...), short names, action buttons, 24px donuts. The information density went way up and the visual noise went way down. That's the trade you want.

Added a `blocLetter` getter to the Topic model — regex pulls from "Ambit A: Entorn", falls back to first char. For expand/collapse state I needed a notifier per exam, and that's where Riverpod 3.x bit me. `StateProvider` has been banished to `legacy.dart` now. Switched to `NotifierProvider.family` with the constructor pattern. Then hit a Dart classic: `{...current}..remove(id)` — the compiler couldn't decide if it was a Map or a Set literal. `Set<String>.of(current)..remove(id)`. One of those things that makes you appreciate languages with fewer curly braces.

Deleted three widget classes. Kept one. The animation is lifted from an existing `CollapsibleBloc` — 300ms controller, RotationTransition on the chevron, AnimatedAlign with heightFactor, ClipRect. Nothing novel. It just works.

Then the polish spiral came back. Stronger this time.

Lightened the topic code badges — killed the filled colored background, went with plain text in muted slate. The filled badges were screaming when the whole point of nesting them inside the accordion was to let them whisper. Added full-width dividers between topic rows. Tried indented dividers first. Edge to edge looked cleaner. Used `expand()` with a guard to skip the divider before the first row.

And then I looked at the whole app and realized the colors were a mess.

Red was everywhere. The flame icon, the PREPARA'T button, the streak dots, the action buttons. Red is the senyera, sure, but when everything is red, nothing is. So I did a sweep. Three files, no new widgets, pure color token swaps. Killed the red accent from main UI surfaces. The flame and PREPARA'T button went navy. The REPASA buttons went navy. The AVALUACIÓ buttons got senyera amber. Streak flame and active dots — amber.

What came out of it wasn't planned. A design language. Navy for study and practice. Amber for evaluation and achievement. Green reserved for success and passed states. Red only for errors and the senyera stripe. I didn't sit down and decide this. It emerged from staring at the screen and asking "why does this button feel wrong." Fifteen color swaps later, there was a system. Sometimes the system finds you.

One hardcoded `#D97706` for the AVALUACIÓ button shadow because there's no dark-amber token in the theme and I'm not adding one for a single use. Code quality is overrated. Ship the thing.

Last thing — the answer options. They'd been doing this thing where correct went solid green and wrong went solid red, like traffic lights screaming at you. Reworked both to soft tinted overlays. Correct gets a light green wash with dark green text and a medium green border. Wrong gets the same treatment in red. All four states — unselected, selected, correct, wrong — now follow the same pattern: tinted background, colored border, colored text, depth shadow always on.

The depth shadow was the detail that made it click. Before, correct and wrong options went flat — zero depth, no border. Now they keep their physicality. The color change tells you what happened. The shadow says the button is still a button. Subtle, but it's the difference between the UI reacting and the UI judging.

There was a casualty. Tried adding a `successBorder` token for a desaturated green border. Looked worse. Reverted with `git checkout`. Which also nuked the font size changes I'd just made. Had to re-apply 14px/w600 manually. The git equivalent of throwing the baby out with the bathwater. Classic.

Four commits in twenty minutes. The kind of flow where you're not thinking about the code, you're thinking about how it looks and the code is just the brush. This is the part of the work that doesn't feel like work. The pixels falling into place.

Some features you build because they're on the roadmap. The streak was one of those. But somewhere between the senyera stripe and the 2AM grace period it stopped being a checkbox and started being mine. The accordion refactor was supposed to be quick. The color sweep wasn't supposed to happen at all. And the option styling was definitely "one more thing" energy at midnight.

That's how the best sessions go. You sit down to do one thing and you end up doing the thing the app actually needed. You just couldn't see it until you started moving.
