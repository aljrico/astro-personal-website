Built the entire streak system in one session. Model, service, provider, widget, database migration, the works. That sentence makes it sound clean. It wasn't. But the pieces fell into place faster than expected and that's the kind of afternoon that reminds you why you do this.

The streak card sits on the home screen now — dark gradient, senyera stripe across the top, seven-day rolling calendar with little Catalan day labels. Dl, Dt, Dc, Dj, Dv, Ds, Dg. There's a 2AM grace period baked into the service because nobody should lose a streak because they were coding until 1:47am. Ask me how I know.

The fun part was the constraint: minimum streak is always 1, never 0. You opened the app. You showed up. That counts. I like that philosophy more than I expected to.

Then the polish spiral started.

Centered today in the week row — three days back, today, three days forward. Duolingo does this and it works because your eye lands on the present. Filled the inactive day circles with a subtle white tint instead of leaving them hollow. Switched to BarlowSemiCondensed at 12px because the day labels were breathing too much.

Tried IEC lowercase labels. dl, dt, dc. Technically more correct. Looked like a whisper. Capitalized them back. Dl, Dt, Dc. Better.

Then I tried swapping the Lucide flame outline for Material's filled `local_fire_department` icon. Hostia, no. It looked like a safety warning on a gas appliance. Reverted in under a minute. Some experiments you run just to confirm your instincts were right.

Found a bug on the way out — the day labels are static, not rolling with actual weekdays. Thursday says Thursday regardless of what day the column actually represents. It's the kind of thing nobody would notice for a week and then it would be all they see. Noted for next time.

Pushed to main. TestFlight build spinning up. The streak card looks good. Not "I need to show everyone" good — more like "this belongs here" good. Quieter satisfaction. The kind where you close the laptop and don't immediately reopen it.

Except I reopened it.

The topics screen had been bugging me. A flat scroll of heavy cards — every topic equally loud, no hierarchy, no sense of structure. The content has blocs. The UI should have blocs. So I tore it out and rebuilt it as collapsible accordions. Bloc supercards with a letter badge, topic count, completion donut, animated chevron. Open one up, the topics fan out as rows — code badge, short name, action buttons, smaller donut. The heavy topic cards are gone. Deleted `_SectionHeader`, `_TagChip`, `_TopicCard`. Kept `_ActionButton` because it did nothing wrong.

Riverpod 3.x bit me on the way in. `StateProvider` has been exiled to `legacy.dart` now, which is Riverpod's polite way of saying "stop using this." Switched to `NotifierProvider.family` with the constructor pattern. Fine. Also hit a Dart set literal ambiguity — `{...current}..remove(id)` looks like a Map to the compiler when it could be a Set. `Set<String>.of(current)..remove(id)` fixed it. The kind of thing that costs five minutes and teaches you something about the language's parser that you'll forget by Friday.

The accordion itself was straightforward — stole the animation pattern from an existing `CollapsibleBloc` I'd built earlier. AnimationController at 300ms, RotationTransition on the chevron, AnimatedAlign with heightFactor for the smooth collapse. Copy-paste inheritance. The best kind.

And then the color spiral.

It started with the topic code badges. They had these filled colored backgrounds that felt heavy inside the accordion rows. Stripped them down to plain text in `colors.muted`. Slate-500 light, slate-400 dark. Quieter. Added dividers between the rows — edge to edge, no indent, because indented dividers inside a card look like the designer couldn't commit.

Then I looked at the home screen and the red was everywhere. The flame icon, the PREPARA'T button, the streak dots. Red is loud. Red says "warning" or "stop" or "error." It shouldn't also say "start practicing." So I killed it. All of it. Red is now reserved for errors and the senyera stripe. That's it.

What replaced it became interesting. The home button went navy. The streak flame and active dots went amber. The REPASA buttons in the topics screen went navy. The AVALUACIÓ buttons went senyera amber with a `#D97706` shadow — amber-600 hardcoded because one button shadow doesn't justify a new theme token.

And suddenly there was a design language. Not planned. Emerged. Navy means study, practice, the daily grind. Amber means evaluation, achievement, the test. Green means you passed. Three colors, three meanings, applied consistently across three files. No new widgets, no new providers. Just color token swaps. But the app feels different. It has opinions now.

After the palette I couldn't stop looking at the answer options. The correct answer was this solid green block — like the app was shouting "RIGHT!" at you. The wrong answer was a solid red block shouting "WRONG!" Both were too much. Answers should feel like information, not judgment.

Softened them both to tinted overlays. Correct gets a pale green background, darker green text, medium green border. Wrong gets the same treatment in red. Both keep their depth shadows — I'd been zeroing those out on answered options and it made them look like they were sinking into the screen. Now all four states are consistent: unselected is white, selected pre-answer is blue tint, correct is green tint, wrong is red tint. All keep borders. All keep shadows. Tinted overlays, not solid color takeovers.

The font weight drop from w700 to w600 was the quietest change and maybe the best one. Options text at 14px/w600 instead of 15px/w700. It breathes.

One moment of joder: tried adding a `successBorder` token for a desaturated green border on correct answers. Looked worse. Reverted with `git checkout`. Which also wiped the font size changes I'd made in the same file. Had to re-apply the 14px/w600 manually. Classic. Version control giveth and version control smacketh you in the face when you get sloppy with your reverts.

Then late afternoon hit and I couldn't leave the evaluation results screen alone. The design language was in my hands now — navy, amber, green — and the results screen was still wearing its old clothes. Generic header, flat layout, no personality.

Ripped the header out and replaced it with a navy block. Stack with a Positioned element bleeding edge to edge, no margin, just solid navy filling the top of the screen. The kind of layout trick that's either elegant or a hack depending on who's reading the code. Below it, an animated donut with the stats — score, time, the numbers that tell you whether those thirty minutes meant something.

The state needed surgery to support this. Added a `selectedAnswers` list running parallel to the questions — every answer tracked, not just the final score. Timestamps too: `sessionStartTime`, `sessionEndTime`, computed props for `sessionDuration` and `averageTimePerQuestion`. The evaluation already knew if you passed. Now it knows how long you agonized over each question. Whether that's useful or just guilt-inducing remains to be seen.

The error review cards were the interesting part. Collapsible, one per wrong answer, with these navy rounded-rect Q-badges — Q1, Q7, Q12 — that tell you which questions you fumbled. Getting the badges to sit inline with the text and answer pills was a `WidgetSpan` wrestling match. Inline wrapping for mixed content — icons next to text next to colored pills — is one of those things Flutter makes possible and unpleasant in equal measure. But it works. The wrong answers fan out with their correct counterparts right there, tinted green and red, the same soft overlays from earlier. Consistency paying dividends already.

Pinned the stats card at the top and let only the error cards scroll beneath it. The score shouldn't move. You should always see it while you're reviewing what went wrong. Small decision, but the kind that makes the screen feel considered instead of generated.

The animated error count in the section title was pure indulgence. A little counter that ticks up to the number of mistakes. Nobody needs this. But it catches the eye and turns "you got 4 wrong" into a moment instead of a label. Added some Catalan copy polish too — "Ara toca repassar i millorar." Now it's time to review and improve. The app talks to you in Catalan now, not at you in English.

Seven commits by end of day. The streak feature was the roadmap item. The accordion was the structural work. The color palette was the session where the app found its visual identity. And the evaluation results — that was the proof that the identity holds. Navy header, amber accents, green for the wins, soft red for the misses. Same language, different screen, still coherent.

Some features you build because they're on the roadmap. The streak was one of those. But somewhere between the senyera stripe and the 2AM grace period it stopped being a checkbox and started being mine. The accordion was structural housekeeping that became satisfying when the animations locked in. The colors were me standing back, squinting at the whole thing, and finally seeing what it wanted to look like. And the evaluation redesign was the first time I applied that vision to a new screen and it just worked. No new palette decisions. No second-guessing. The language was already there. I just spoke it.

Four kinds of work in one day. The build, the refactor, the taste, and the application of taste. That last one doesn't happen unless the first three went well. Today they did.
