The game screen finally has a sky.

It was supposed to all along. The home screen has this choreographed backdrop and the game screen was meant to share it, but it never showed. Turned out to be two bugs stacked on top of each other: an opaque ColoredBox painting over everything, and underneath that, the loading screen's ink base just... never leaving the widget stack. Fixed the first one, still no sky. That's the worst moment, when the fix that should work doesn't, because it means your mental model is wrong. Then found the second culprit and the whole thing dissolved into a crossfade and hostia, there it was. The board floating on the sky. I sat there watching it for a while.

Undo shipped too. A bounded stack of a hundred snapshots, recording hooks inside the placement and notes controllers. Rejected placements record nothing. The auto note-tidy restores atomically, so one undo brings your notes back with the piece. It feels right when you use it, which is the only spec that matters.

All the chrome buttons converged on the same ghost-cream square. The back chevron is the forward chevron flipped with a negative scale, because drawing a second SVG for a mirror image is for people with more patience than me.

Then the commit, which took longer than some of the features. The tree was carrying two and a half thousand lines of a different session's island work, so committing "just tonight" meant surgery: cherry-picking the undo hunks out of fifteen dirty localization files, verifying the result standalone in a temp worktree. Flutter also lost the device twice mid-session and I had to fish the VM URL out of the simulator logs to re-attach. Pushed anyway. Venga.

The afternoon was the truth session. A real per-country AdMob export landed (UTF-16, tab-separated, because Google) and replaced my eCPM estimates with actual dollars. The estimates had been flattering me by up to 24 percent. The US was doubly flattered: stale eCPM plus AdMob attributing a third fewer American impressions than my own events claim. Probably VPNs. The US premium collapsed from 4.3x Spain to 2.5x. Germany turned out to be a quiet earner. Russia monetizes at exactly zero dollars.

Blended D30 LTV: eleven cents. I keep turning that number over. It's honest now, which is worth more than it being bigger. But still. Eleven cents.

The consolation was retention. D7 firmed up at 12.1% on almost six thousand users, which means last week's scary refit was 169 users of noise. I panicked over a sample size I'd have laughed at in another life. The physicist knew better. The indie dev refit the curve anyway.

Past midnight I was still up, adding an info button next to SUBMIT. It did nothing. Tapping it produced nothing, no action wired yet. But the icon was right: traced a pasted sketch through a potrace pipeline, punched the "i" through the badge as actual transparent holes instead of faked white fill. Lost a chunk of the night to flutter_svg silently caching the parsed asset, so hot reload kept showing me stale art and I kept editing a file that was already correct. The trivial ones always cost the most.

A button that looks perfect and does nothing. At 1am that felt like a reasonable place to stop.

I didn't stop. By 2:30 the button had learned what it's for: a proper rules sheet, same bottom-sheet language as settings. Three sections, the basics, reading clues, the islands, with the island section gated behind actually having reached the islands, because nothing teaches worse than explaining a mode you can't play yet. The little example boards are declarative configs now, describe a grid, get a mini, and the animated ones reuse the tutorial's scene loop. Before writing a word of copy I grepped the real clue strings across all seven locales, so the sheet teaches in exactly the vocabulary the clues speak. If the clue says at the water's edge, the rules say at the water's edge.

And then writing the manual found a bug in the game. Halfway through explaining what at the water's edge means, I realized the engine and I disagreed. I always meant sand only, the beach cell touching water. The engine was counting both sides of the coast, water cells included. Months of play never surfaced it. One paragraph of teaching copy surfaced it in ten minutes. Fixed the predicate, fixed the generator so water beside sand gets no distance clue instead of falling through to a false open-water, re-pinned the tests, re-baked all eighteen island campaign seeds. Turns out player-facing copy is the best semantics audit I own.

By three I was making the rule minis render real terrain, pulling the sand autotile mask out of the actual island board painter so the teaching boards stack the same water and autotiled sand sprites as the real game. No diagrams, no approximations. The actual art. Checked that every mini's mask resolves to a sprite in the 47-tile set, because at 3am you trust nothing, least of all yourself.

The shared tree, the one that forced surgery at commit time, behaved like choreography by then. Two sessions working the same repo all day. The other one's evening commits swept up my uncommitted rules-sheet files (worked out, this time), and I held back my own commit because it would have collided with a refactor that hadn't landed yet. Minutes later it landed, and mine went in clean, two files. The parallel-session rules earned their keep.

Slept. Briefly.

Mid-afternoon, back at the desk, the teaching copy claimed two more victims. The inland and open-water clues, the ones that grade how far from the coast a piece sits. They've always been awkward to explain, and writing the rules sheet made me admit why: they were never in the design. The island vocabulary was supposed to be three ideas, on land, in water, at the water's edge, plus the map sides. The zone clues crept in because they helped generation yield. This time I let the physicist drive before deleting anything: 64 samples across nine geometry profiles. Cutting them costs 19 to 31 points of yield on multi-island 6x6 and 7x7 boards, worst case 95 down to 64 percent, and nothing anywhere else. Painful, but nowhere near the 51-to-8 collapse the spatial clues threatened when I once tried letting those in. The runtime cost is a fatter search tail on the big dynamic boards, and the 1.3-second loading beat swallows it. So they're gone, gated out at config level with a one-line rollback if I regret it. All eighteen campaign levels re-baked to exact ramp targets. Suite green.

The shore teaching row collapsed along with them, from an animated three-phase zones demo to a static mini that says the edge is the sand ring beside the water. Simpler copy in all seven locales. The rules sheet is shorter than the one I wrote at 2am and the game is better for it.

Twice in twelve hours, the same lesson. First the teaching copy caught a clue whose meaning had drifted from what I meant. Then it killed two clues that couldn't be explained without a diagram and an apology. If a rule can't survive being written down in a sentence a player will actually read, it isn't a rule, it's an implementation detail with delusions. The button that did nothing turned out to be the harshest reviewer this game has ever had.

After all that theory, the late afternoon went to the fun stuff. Visual work. The place flow actually lives for me — not the yield analysis, not the forensics. Making things look right.

Phone landscape first. The board had been sitting in a fixed split left over from the era when the screen scrolled: dead margins on both sides, the rail squeezing its labels down to "Coloc…" and "Hild…". Made it behave like the tablet does — board takes the full height, flush left, rail gets every remaining pixel. The absurd part is verification: landscape simulator screenshots come out as portrait images with the content rotated, so I checked my work by spinning every screenshot ninety degrees before looking at it. You get used to it. That's the disturbing part.

Then a proper header. Title, timer, and a little scene miniature on one ghost-cream panel, same visual language as the buttons, shared between portrait and both landscape rails, folding away on victory. Flutter charged its toll twice: IntrinsicHeight silently blanks any LayoutBuilder underneath it — no error, no warning, just an empty subtree — and SizeTransition has an internal Align that inflates to whatever height you hand it and pins your child to the ceiling. Two rounds of "why is the icon up there" for what is, in the end, a rectangle with a clock on it. But the rectangle is right now. The rectangle is very right.

Then words. The islands casebook lore had been lazy, placeholder prose sitting next to Murder Next Door's blurbs, which actually commit to the bit. The direction that settled: locale-native comedy, references as garnish, not skeleton. The machine's first drafts came back too forced — pastiche with the labels still showing — and the best line of the whole set I ended up writing myself: "Calas de postal, hogueras al atardecer e imágenes que nadie quiere ver. De esta experiencia uno no sale vivo." There's something in that. The agent re-bakes eighteen campaign seeds without blinking, but a joke in your own language still has to come from a person. I find that weirdly comforting. We also nearly credited an ANHQV line to Paquita Salas, which in Spain is the kind of mistake you don't recover from socially. Caught it. Every casebook has a comedy patron saint now, and the Catalan one got Crims threaded through it — a "posar llum a la foscor" folded into a placement pun here, a dry "Comencem." as a closer there. One reference per slot, never two. Restraint is what separates homage from cosplay.

Then the name. "Island Mysteries" was a placeholder wearing a trench coat. Riffed out loud — death island? murder island? something piratey? — and landed on Dead Man's Cove. Crime word plus idyllic place, the same house formula as Murder Next Door. The other locales got native placenames instead of translations: La cala del muerto. Die Totenbucht. Dodemansbaai. Each one sounds like somewhere your grandmother told you not to swim. Redrew the cover art twice before it matched the name's energy. The new one is a 2.2-megabyte PNG, which is obscene, and I will compress it approximately never.

Then, mid-commit on that cover, a cold little thought: after the clue purge, do existing installs actually get new puzzles? Drove the simulator to CASO 6 and there it was. "Tierra adentro." The inland clue. The one I killed two hours ago. Alive, in the shipped content, pointing at a piece.

The disk cache stores full baked puzzles, not seeds, and none of today's content changes had bumped its version. Every existing device would have replayed pre-gating fossils forever — clues from a ruleset that no longer exists, immune to every re-bake, invisible to the ship-gate tests because those regenerate from seed and never look in the graveyard. One version bump and CASO 6 regenerated clean: new layout, new cast, the clue now reads "en tierra." So the day's lesson got a third act. I retired a clue at lunch and it came back before dinner like a ghost. Kill the rule, then check the cemetery.

And then — I want this on the record, because the morning version of me would not believe it — I covered the sky back up.

The sky. The one I fought two stacked bugs to reveal. After a full day of actually playing on it, the clouds drifting behind the clue text were noise, not magic. So the flat cream surface went back behind the board, one ColoredBox. The route stays transparent, so the board still flies in across the sky — the sky is choreography now, not wallpaper. It's the right call and I'm only slightly bitter. The embarrassing part was working out what "the background we used to have" even was: three candidates from three eras of this game, including an entire village painting that has been sitting in the assets since forever, never once drawn on screen. A whole background nobody ever saw. There's a metaphor in there I refuse to examine today.

Last stretch: the hint chips. They were teal and they were wrong. Now they're ghost-cream pills like everything else, with a permanent lightbulb and an actual entrance — elastic pop around the bulb, a stretch, then the clue types itself out, one 850-millisecond controller running all three phases. Only fresh reveals animate; old hints just sit there like they've always known. Getting the capture right meant slowing the whole app with timeDilation, which turns out to be a process-wide global: I deleted the line and the app stayed underwater, every transition in slow motion, until I reset it explicitly. And converting the widget to stateful is a change hot reload cannot survive, so the restart ate the case I'd been play-testing all afternoon. The game keeps getting murdered by its own polishing. Thematically consistent, at least.

The test suite caught a delayed casualty too: the pretty header from two hours earlier had quietly broken nine tests. Taller header, tray and SUBMIT pushed below the fold of the tiny test viewport, taps landing on nothing with only hit-test warnings to show for it. Silent misses are the worst kind. Bisected it across four checkouts in a scratch worktree to be sure it was really the header, added scroll-into-view before the taps, 228 green, pushed.

All evening the two sessions kept evicting each other's builds from the simulator like flatmates with one shower. Twice I rebuilt; once I deliberately didn't, to leave the other one's test build alone. Last night's parallel-session rules keep paying rent.

Stopped before eight. An actual evening stop, not a 3am collapse. The suite is green, the casebook has a name that sounds like a warning, the hints have an entrance, and somewhere out there an existing player's CASO 6 will quietly regenerate into a puzzle that obeys rules I can actually explain. The eleven cents from the truth session are still eleven cents. But the game those cents are measuring got better today in a dozen ways no dashboard will ever show. That's either the whole point of this or an elaborate cope. Ask me in the morning.
