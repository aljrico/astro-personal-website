The game screen finally has a sky.

It was supposed to all along. The home screen has this choreographed backdrop and the game screen was meant to share it, but it never showed. Turned out to be two bugs stacked on top of each other: an opaque ColoredBox painting over everything, and underneath that, the loading screen's ink base just... never leaving the widget stack. Fixed the first one, still no sky. That's the worst moment, when the fix that should work doesn't, because it means your mental model is wrong. Then found the second culprit and the whole thing dissolved into a crossfade and hostia, there it was. The board floating on the sky. I sat there watching it for a while.

Undo shipped too. A bounded stack of a hundred snapshots, recording hooks inside the placement and notes controllers. Rejected placements record nothing. The auto note-tidy restores atomically, so one undo brings your notes back with the piece. It feels right when you use it, which is the only spec that matters.

All the chrome buttons converged on the same ghost-cream square. The back chevron is the forward chevron flipped with a negative scale, because drawing a second SVG for a mirror image is for people with more patience than me.

Then the commit, which took longer than some of the features. The tree was carrying two and a half thousand lines of a different session's island work, so committing "just tonight" meant surgery: cherry-picking the undo hunks out of fifteen dirty localization files, verifying the result standalone in a temp worktree. Flutter also lost the device twice mid-session and I had to fish the VM URL out of the simulator logs to re-attach. Pushed anyway. Venga.

The afternoon was the truth session. A real per-country AdMob export landed (UTF-16, tab-separated, because Google) and replaced my eCPM estimates with actual dollars. The estimates had been flattering me by up to 24 percent. The US was doubly flattered: stale eCPM plus AdMob attributing a third fewer American impressions than my own events claim. Probably VPNs. The US premium collapsed from 4.3x Spain to 2.5x. Germany turned out to be a quiet earner. Russia monetizes at exactly zero dollars.

Blended D30 LTV: eleven cents. I keep turning that number over. It's honest now, which is worth more than it being bigger. But still. Eleven cents.

The consolation was retention. D7 firmed up at 12.1% on almost six thousand users, which means last week's scary refit was 169 users of noise. I panicked over a sample size I'd have laughed at in another life. The physicist knew better. The indie dev refit the curve anyway.

Past midnight I was still up, adding an info button next to SUBMIT. It did nothing. Tapping it produced nothing, no action wired yet. But the icon was right: traced a pasted sketch through a potrace pipeline, punched the "i" through the badge as actual transparent holes instead of faked white fill. Lost a chunk of the night to flutter_svg silently caching the parsed asset, so hot reload kept showing me stale art and I kept editing a file that was already correct. The trivial ones always cost the most.

A button that looks perfect and does nothing. At 1am that felt like a reasonable place to stop.

I didn't stop.

By 2:30 the button had learned what it's for. It opens a rules sheet now, settings-style, three sections: the basics, reading clues, the islands. That last one only appears once you've actually reached the islands, because explaining a mechanic you haven't met yet is just a spoiler with extra steps. Each rule gets a mini board next to it, and the minis are declarative little configs, not screenshots. The animated ones reuse the tutorial's scene loop. Building the sheet was the easy part.

The interesting part was what writing the copy did. Somewhere around "some clues tell you how close you are to the water's edge" I stopped, because I couldn't write the sentence cleanly. The engine was counting BOTH sides of the coastline, water cells included, but the intent had always been sand-only. The code had drifted from the design and nobody noticed, because code doesn't have to explain itself. A sentence does. Fixed the predicate, fixed the generator so water beside sand doesn't fall through into a false clue, re-baked all eighteen island campaign levels. And before writing a word of the copy itself, I grepped the clue strings across all seven languages so the rules sheet uses exactly the vocabulary the clues use. If the clue says "at the water's edge," the rule that explains it better say those exact words, in Catalan too.

By 3:15 the minis were rendering real game terrain. Pulled the sand autotiling mask out of the board painter and made it public, so now the little rule diagrams stack actual water sprites and the exact autotiled sand edge the real board would draw. The rules page shows the same coastline the game shows. Small thing. Sat there admiring it anyway, same as the sky.

Meanwhile there were two sessions working the same repo all day, and it turned into a small game of air traffic control. My rules-sheet files got swept up into the other session's island commits at one point. Later I had to hold a commit because it would have collided with a refactor the other session hadn't landed yet, and then it landed minutes later and unblocked me cleanly. It all worked out, but the margin was luck as much as discipline.

Then sleep, finally. And by mid-afternoon I was back in it, because the rules sheet wasn't done auditing the game.

Two of the island clues, "inland" and "in open water," had been awkward to explain all night. Every phrasing came out lawyerly. This afternoon I stopped trying and asked the real question: should these clues exist? Gated them out of generation entirely. But measured, not vibed: I ran the experiment tool first, 64 samples across nine geometry profiles. Removing the zones costs 19 to 31 points of generation yield on the nasty multi-island boards, worst case 95 down to 64 percent. Real money, but nothing like the bloodbath the last clue-family experiment produced. The cost is a fatter search tail on device, and the loading animation is 1.3 seconds long anyway. It hides there. Accepted.

Re-baked all eighteen campaign levels again, second time in twelve hours, every ramp target hit exactly. Then went back to the rules sheet and collapsed the shore row: the fancy animated three-phase mini I'd built at 2am existed to teach a distinction that no longer exists. It became a static picture: the edge is the sand ring beside the water. Simplified the copy across all seven languages. Killing your own six-hour-old work should hurt more than it does.

So the rules sheet was supposed to be documentation. A politeness. Instead it spent a full day interrogating the game: first it caught a clue whose meaning had quietly drifted from the design, then it executed two clues whose explanations wouldn't come out clean no matter how I held them. Turns out writing "here's how this works" for a player is the harshest code review there is. If the sentence is awkward, the mechanic is awkward. The copy was never the problem.

The island vocabulary is back to what it was always meant to be: on land, in water, at the water's edge. Three phrases a tired person on a bus can hold in their head. The game got simpler because I tried to explain it. I should try explaining things more often.
