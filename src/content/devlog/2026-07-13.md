The game screen finally has a sky.

It was supposed to all along. The home screen has this choreographed backdrop and the game screen was meant to share it, but it never showed. Turned out to be two bugs stacked on top of each other: an opaque ColoredBox painting over everything, and underneath that, the loading screen's ink base just... never leaving the widget stack. Fixed the first one, still no sky. That's the worst moment, when the fix that should work doesn't, because it means your mental model is wrong. Then found the second culprit and the whole thing dissolved into a crossfade and hostia, there it was. The board floating on the sky. I sat there watching it for a while.

Undo shipped too. A bounded stack of a hundred snapshots, recording hooks inside the placement and notes controllers. Rejected placements record nothing. The auto note-tidy restores atomically, so one undo brings your notes back with the piece. It feels right when you use it, which is the only spec that matters.

All the chrome buttons converged on the same ghost-cream square. The back chevron is the forward chevron flipped with a negative scale, because drawing a second SVG for a mirror image is for people with more patience than me.

Then the commit, which took longer than some of the features. The tree was carrying two and a half thousand lines of a different session's island work, so committing "just tonight" meant surgery: cherry-picking the undo hunks out of fifteen dirty localization files, verifying the result standalone in a temp worktree. Flutter also lost the device twice mid-session and I had to fish the VM URL out of the simulator logs to re-attach. Pushed anyway. Venga.

The afternoon was the truth session. A real per-country AdMob export landed (UTF-16, tab-separated, because Google) and replaced my eCPM estimates with actual dollars. The estimates had been flattering me by up to 24 percent. The US was doubly flattered: stale eCPM plus AdMob attributing a third fewer American impressions than my own events claim. Probably VPNs. The US premium collapsed from 4.3x Spain to 2.5x. Germany turned out to be a quiet earner. Russia monetizes at exactly zero dollars.

Blended D30 LTV: eleven cents. I keep turning that number over. It's honest now, which is worth more than it being bigger. But still. Eleven cents.

The consolation was retention. D7 firmed up at 12.1% on almost six thousand users, which means last week's scary refit was 169 users of noise. I panicked over a sample size I'd have laughed at in another life. The physicist knew better. The indie dev refit the curve anyway.

Past midnight I was still up, adding an info button next to SUBMIT. It did nothing. Tapping it produced nothing, no action wired yet. But the icon was right: traced a pasted sketch through a potrace pipeline, punched the "i" through the badge as actual transparent holes instead of faked white fill. Lost a chunk of the night to flutter_svg silently caching the parsed asset, so hot reload kept showing me stale art and I kept editing a file that was already correct. The trivial ones always cost the most.

A button that looks perfect and does nothing. At 1am that felt like a reasonable place to stop.

I didn't stop. By 2:30 the button had learned what it's for: a proper rules sheet, same bottom-sheet language as settings. Three sections, the basics, reading clues, the islands, with the island section gated behind actually having reached the islands, because nothing teaches worse than explaining a mode you can't play yet. The little example boards are declarative configs now, describe a grid, get a mini, and the animated ones reuse the tutorial's scene loop. Before writing a word of copy I grepped the real clue strings across all seven locales, so the sheet teaches in exactly the vocabulary the clues speak. If the clue says at the water's edge, the rules say at the water's edge.

And then writing the manual found a bug in the game. Halfway through explaining what at the water's edge means, I realized the engine and I disagreed. I always meant sand only, the beach cell touching water. The engine was counting both sides of the coast, water cells included. Months of play never surfaced it. One paragraph of teaching copy surfaced it in ten minutes. Fixed the predicate, fixed the generator so water beside sand gets no distance clue instead of falling through to a false open-water, re-pinned the tests, re-baked all eighteen island campaign seeds. Turns out player-facing copy is the best semantics audit I own.

By three I was making the rule minis render real terrain, pulling the sand autotile mask out of the actual island board painter so the teaching boards stack the same water and autotiled sand sprites as the real game. No diagrams, no approximations. The actual art. Checked that every mini's mask resolves to a sprite in the 47-tile set, because at 3am you trust nothing, least of all yourself.

The shared tree, the one that forced surgery at commit time, behaved like choreography by then. Two sessions working the same repo all day. The other one's evening commits swept up my uncommitted rules-sheet files (worked out, this time), and I held back my own commit because it would have collided with a refactor that hadn't landed yet. Minutes later it landed, and mine went in clean, two files. The parallel-session rules earned their keep.

Slept. Briefly.

Mid-afternoon, back at the desk, the teaching copy claimed two more victims. The inland and open-water clues, the ones that grade how far from the coast a piece sits. They've always been awkward to explain, and writing the rules sheet made me admit why: they were never in the design. The island vocabulary was supposed to be three ideas, on land, in water, at the water's edge, plus the map sides. The zone clues crept in because they helped generation yield. This time I let the physicist drive before deleting anything: 64 samples across nine geometry profiles. Cutting them costs 19 to 31 points of yield on multi-island 6x6 and 7x7 boards, worst case 95 down to 64 percent, and nothing anywhere else. Painful, but nowhere near the 51-to-8 collapse the spatial clues threatened when I once tried letting those in. The runtime cost is a fatter search tail on the big dynamic boards, and the 1.3-second loading beat swallows it. So they're gone, gated out at config level with a one-line rollback if I regret it. All eighteen campaign levels re-baked to exact ramp targets. Suite green.

The shore teaching row collapsed along with them, from an animated three-phase zones demo to a static mini that says the edge is the sand ring beside the water. Simpler copy in all seven locales. The rules sheet is shorter than the one I wrote at 2am and the game is better for it.

Twice in twelve hours, the same lesson. First the teaching copy caught a clue whose meaning had drifted from what I meant. Then it killed two clues that couldn't be explained without a diagram and an apology. If a rule can't survive being written down in a sentence a player will actually read, it isn't a rule, it's an implementation detail with delusions. The button that did nothing turned out to be the harshest reviewer this game has ever had.

The rest of the daylight went to polish. My favorite drug.

First, phone landscape. Rotating the phone got you a letterboxed board with dead margins on both sides and a controls rail so starved it truncated its own labels. "Coloc...". "Hild...". The tablet solved this properly ages ago, board sized to height, rail takes everything else, and the phone was still living on a fixed 3:2 split left over from the era when the screen scrolled. Made the phone behave like the tablet, plus one guard the tablet never needed: cap the board at three fifths of the width so the rail survives split-screen. Verifying it was its own small comedy, because simulator screenshots of a landscape device come out as portrait images with the content rotated inside them. You rotate the screenshot to look at the screenshot.

Then the header. Title, timer, and the scene miniature sit together now on one flat ghost-cream panel, same language as the buttons, shared across portrait and both landscape rails, and it folds itself away on victory. Flutter fought me twice. IntrinsicHeight silently blanks any LayoutBuilder underneath it, so instead of measuring anything, the miniature size is a constant with a derivation: 24 of title plus 3 of gap plus 17.5 of timer badge plus padding equals 48.5. A number computed by hand because the framework refused to compute it for me. And SizeTransition secretly wraps its child in an Align that expands to whatever height you give it, so the portrait toolbar was inflating the panel and pinning the icon to the ceiling. An explicit SizedBox ended the argument. Titles are all-caps at render now instead of hoping seven arb files agree.

Then the words. The neighborhood casebook's Spanish blurbs are a little parody machine, each slot riffing on a different register, and the island blurbs sitting next to them were lazy. Translated, not written. So: locale-native comedy, references as garnish, never as skeleton. The drafts came back too forced, jokes you could hear straining, and I ended up writing the Tentaciones line myself: "Calas de postal, hogueras al atardecer e imágenes que nadie quiere ver. De esta experiencia uno no sale vivo." The Catalan pass got true-crime-TV solemnity woven through it, one Crims catchphrase per slot, never two. Also learned I'd misattributed my own reference: "un poquito de por favor" is Aquí no hay quien viva, not Paquita Salas. Documented every divergence in the English arb so some future translation pass doesn't flatten the jokes back into translations.

And the islands casebook finally lost its placeholder name. "Island Mysteries" always sounded like shovelware. I riffed out loud, death island, murder island, something piratey?, and landed on pirate-legend placename energy: Dead Man's Cove. The boards have no ships or parrots, only skulls and geography, so full pirate would be a lie; a postcard place with a body in its name is exactly right. Same house formula as Murder Next Door, a crime word bolted to an idyllic place. And per locale it's not a translation, it's a native placename: La cala del muerto, La cala del mort, Die Totenbucht, Бухта мерця. They should get a native speaker's eye eventually. For now they all sound like places where you'd find exactly one skeleton. Gave it a punchier cover to match, a two-megabyte PNG for a thumbnail, which is obscene, a problem for a day when I care about app size.

Then, mid-commit on that cover art, the fossil. I was play-testing CASO 6 on the sim and there it was on screen: "tierra adentro." A clue I deleted at lunch, alive at dinner. Joder. The disk cache stores the full baked puzzle, and both of today's clue changes altered what the generator produces without bumping the cache version. Which means every device that ever opened those cases would replay pre-purge fossils forever, and the ship-gate tests were blind to it because they regenerate from seed instead of reading a cache. One version bump, cache invalidated, CASO 6 came back with a new layout, a new cast, and a legal clue. Progress survives, it's versioned separately. Third lesson from the same clue work in one day: deleting a rule from the generator doesn't delete it from the world. The world has caches.

Somewhere in there I also buried the sky. The one I won this morning with two stacked bug fixes. The one I sat and watched. Playing real cases all evening made it undeniable: clouds drifting under the board read as noise, not atmosphere. So the game screen sits on flat cream again, one ColoredBox at the base of the stack, and the sky only exists for the second the push transition rides through it. Deciding which background to restore took actual archaeology, three candidates from three eras of the game stacked like sediment: a caramel tile from 1.0, a village painting that never got wired up, the flat surface. Flat surface won. It stings to kill the morning's trophy before the day is even over. It's also exactly the kind of call you get to make when the game is yours.

The last push was the hint chips. They were teal, the only teal left standing, so they joined the ghost-cream family: pills with a permanent lightbulb. Then choreography, because a revealed hint should feel like a reveal: elastic pop around the bulb, a stretch out to the right, then the text types itself in, one 850-millisecond controller running all three acts. The typewriter keeps the untyped tail transparent instead of absent, so the pill never reflows mid-sentence. Only fresh reveals animate. Reopening a case doesn't replay the show.

Three gotchas took their tax on the way. Converting the widget to stateful is the one change hot reload can't survive, so the restart ate the case I was mid-solving. Then, to capture the animation frames, I slowed the whole app down with timeDilation, which turns out to be a process-wide global that cheerfully outlives the line that set it: I deleted the line and the app stayed underwater, every transition oozing, until I set it back explicitly. And the pill text overflowed because a Row hands its children infinite width. Flexible. Always Flexible. The verification rig was the fun part: driving the simulator tap by tap from the command line, quarter-speed time, screenshot bursts. A little film set for an 850-millisecond shot.

Then the suite got its revenge for the header panel. Nine tests red, and not honestly red: the taller header pushes SUBMIT below the fold in the tests' little 800x600 window, and a tap on an offscreen widget doesn't fail, it quietly hits nothing and lets some assertion downstream die confused. Bisected it in a scratch worktree to prove it was the panel and not the pills. The fix was mundane, scroll the thing into view before tapping it, seven call sites. 228 green. Pushed.

The shared tree demanded manners all evening too: staging localization files strictly by path after grepping the diff for the other session's lines, and the simulator, which only holds one debug build, watched us evict each other twice like two people fighting over one TV remote.

By the time the suite went green it was dark and the game looked more like one object than it ever has. Back chevron, header panel, hint pills, all the same ghost cream, like one hand made it. Eleven cents of LTV and the best this game has ever looked, both true on the same day. I know which number I should be turning over. I know which screen I'll actually dream about.
