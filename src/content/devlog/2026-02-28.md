Came back to Hollows in the afternoon and did the thing I almost never do: closed the book.

Cleaned up the lore bible, killed a stale TODO that had already been fixed weeks ago. Then looked at the Phase 7 design — trap node, cursed altar — and scrapped it. Six node types are enough. Those two added complexity without adding fun, which is the worst kind of complexity. The kind that exists because you planned it, not because the game needs it.

Hollows is feature-complete.

Except it wasn't, quite. Ran a full code review sweep of the entire Hollows codebase and pulled three real bugs out of what I thought was finished code. Toll payments weren't atomic — validating and deducting costs one at a time, so a failed check halfway through could leave you with partial resource loss. A retain cycle hiding in CombatFlowController's nested asyncAfter, the classic missing `[weak self]`. And a chest typewriter effect spawning N fire-and-forget async calls when a single cancellable Task would do. The rest of the review was false alarms, but those three were real. The kind of bugs that work fine when you're testing because you never hit the edge case, then silently rot in production.

*Now* Hollows is feature-complete.

I keep waiting for the satisfaction to land. It doesn't. What lands instead is a restless scan of everything else that isn't done.

So I looked at ascension. The late-game wall had been nagging me — that feeling where prestige stops mattering. Dug into the numbers and found the structural problem: only 14 out of roughly 93 buildings have ascension boosts. The rest return a flat 1. No global prestige multiplier. So after a few ascensions, production plateaus and the whole loop deflates.

The fix was simple. A +1% global production bonus per total ascension, applied as a BigNumber multiplier in the core productivity calculation. Small enough that early game doesn't notice, cumulative enough that late game feels alive again. Added a view for it on the ascension cloud tab — squircle background, left-aligned, gated on having ascended at least once. Clean.

Then the layout broke. The Halo view was inflating the ZStack frame, pushing the bottom card off screen. I was messing with Spacer hacks trying to force it back. The agent caught the real cause — moved Halo to a `.background()` modifier and everything snapped into place. Sometimes you need someone else to see the obvious thing.

But the bigger problem was the cost curve. Ascension upgrades scaled linearly, and I initially thought: just make them flat. Use the initial cost, done. The physicist brain wouldn't let it go. Ran the math. Flat cost diverges — 2^n multiplier upgrades outpace the 1.048^T requirement by roughly 5x in log-space. Rewards go 147 → 251 → 1149 → 5999, geometrically. A flat cost curve means the player hits a "buy everything instantly" phase within a few cycles, and then the system is dead.

The divergence is structural. Exponential multiplier versus polynomial cost. Not a tuning knob — a fundamentally different curve shape was needed.

Sqrt scaling. `initialCost * ceil(sqrt(1 + owned))`. Converges around 68 levels after roughly 10 ascension cycles. Early game gets the "wow, upgrades are cheap" dopamine. Late game hits equilibrium. The ceil rounding keeps displayed costs as clean integers, no fractional weirdness. It's the kind of solution that feels inevitable after you find it and invisible before.

Took a detour into the business side. Pulled the full spending data for January-February via SQL — gems, IAP, the works.

The gem economy deflated about 70% after removing the noads-via-gems path in v5. The permanent boost card briefly spiked then settled around 9K gems per week. Expected. What wasn't expected: one user accounts for 71% of all TilesMatch gem spending. 3,254 transactions in two months. A whale in a sea of minnows. The skip_expedition sink that appeared late February — also whale-driven, 92% from a single user.

There's something uncomfortable about building systems where the economics depend on one or two people. The math works but it's fragile in a way that doesn't show up in averages.

Added source tracking to ad analytics while I was in there — an `analyticsSource` property on the AdUnit enum, a `logAdWatched(source:)` function that tags which feature triggered each ad view. Nine sources total. Applied the Supabase migration. Wrote the analysis up properly for future reference. Committed, pushed.

Then 23:00 hit and the mad scientist showed up.

The idea was simple: FlamaCoach needs current affairs questions for the Mossos exam. Not from legal PDFs — from actual news. 3cat.cat publishes Catalan news with a paginated API. The questions should feel fresh. "What happened last week" type stuff, difficulty D1 through D3. No deep legal analysis, just awareness.

Three new Python scripts. Fetch headlines from the 3cat API with pagination. Score them for exam relevance using OpenAI batch. Fetch the article bodies, strip the HTML, shape them into the exact same chunk format the legal pipeline already uses.

That last part is the whole trick.

The chunks come out looking identical to legal document chunks. Same schema, same fields. Which means the entire downstream pipeline — question generation, topic grouping, DB build — just works. No changes. I plugged news content into a system designed for law PDFs and it didn't notice the difference. Three new scripts at the top, zero changes at the bottom.

Added a `--prompt-variant actualitat` flag to the question generator with a news-specific system prompt. Told it to avoid ephemeral details that expire in a week. Added `mossos_actualitat` as an exam config for topic grouping. Added an `EXAM_SOURCES` registry and `--add-exam` flag to the DB builder so multiple content sources can coexist in one SQLite file.

The code review caught a real bug — `--max-articles` was slicing the list before filtering by relevance score, silently dropping valid articles. The kind of bug that works fine in testing and fails quietly in production. Caught it. Fixed it.

Had a brief detour into token estimation math. How many tokens per word for Catalan news text? 0.75? 1.3? Kept the ratio loose because the articles are short and the chunking is just a safety net anyway. The physicist brain wanted to nail down the number. The builder brain said ship it. Builder won.

The whole pipeline went from nothing to committed in one session. Fetch → score → fetch bodies → generate questions → group topics → build DB. End to end. All reusing existing infrastructure except the three ingestion scripts.

This is the part I actually love about pipelines. Not the individual scripts — those are boring. It's the moment you realize the abstraction you chose six weeks ago was the right one. That the chunk schema was general enough. That "just make everything look the same by the time it hits the question generator" was the right bet. You don't know if your architecture is good until something unexpected needs to fit through it. News fit. Didn't even have to push.

Venga. February's last day. Hollows closed — properly this time, bugs and all. Ascension math sorted. A whale spotted. A pipeline built. One thing finished, three things started. Tomorrow it's a new month and I still haven't fixed whatever I was supposed to fix before I built all this instead.
