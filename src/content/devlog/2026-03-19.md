Started the afternoon hunting a fat tail in gem rewards. The min-of-K distribution was producing absurd outliers — upper bound of 67 gems at Tier 8. The kind of number that makes your economy look generous until someone screenshots it and posts "free gems exploit." Nerfed it hard: pushed K up, dropped the exponent from 1.25 to linear. Probability of 30+ gems cut by 20x. Math problem solved in math. That part felt clean.

Then the tentacles.

Pop animation: death wiggle, opacity fade, floating reward numbers in dark green. The kind of polish that takes twenty minutes to describe and four hours to get right because SwiftUI keeps finding new ways to crash when your @State array changes size mid-render. Index out of range on launch. Fixed it, Codex caught that my fix didn't prevent first-frame crashes either. Fixed it again with `.id()`. The cycle of confidence and correction that defines working with two AIs reviewing your code while you're trying to ship.

The real surgery was SpriteKit. Two stacked full-screen transparent SpriteViews — CurrencyJuiceScene and FlyingDecorationScene — were causing a Metal compositing bug that broke subsequent animations. Traced it back through commits: one scene got the always-mounted fix, the other didn't. Classic. The kind of bug where the root cause is a decision made three weeks ago that seemed reasonable at the time.

Merged them into one scene. Renamed it EffectsOverlayScene because that's what it actually is — a single animation bus. Extracted falling cookies from SpriteKit entirely into a UIKit CALayer. Render-server driven, zero main-thread cost. Sometimes the best optimization is admitting you picked the wrong tool.

The coordinate conversion for crate landing positions was its own saga. Tried GridFrame math. Tried scale/offset transforms. Tried GeometryReader. All wrong. The fix was embarrassingly obvious: use SpriteKit's own `convertPoint(toView:)`. Lesson I keep relearning — use the rendering engine's coordinate system, not yours.

Somewhere in there I also discovered that tentacle pops were calling `earnCoins` instead of `earn`. Giving coins instead of cookies. Nobody noticed because both numbers go up and dopamine doesn't check the currency type.

---

Killed an AB test. offlineProductionDesign_v1 — the treatment halved noads IAP conversion. Ads revenue ticked up slightly but nowhere near enough to compensate. But the interesting finding was underneath: version-over-version analysis showed the gem sink collapsed. Expedition skip demand dropped 150x. A hundred and fifty times. Because the layer system resets tech tree depth, bosses spawn too early, skips are cheap. The economy ate itself.

Pushed boss spawns deeper. Built a gem X3 chest boost button with upsell flow. Wired a new AB test to replace the dead one.

The toll node had been checking cookies instead of coins. Naming confusion — wealth, coins, cookies, three words for things that are almost but not quite the same thing. Added a rejection animation: wiggle, red halo, error haptic. If you can't afford it, the game should make you *feel* it.

Tentacle squish sounds had multi-second delays because MainScene idles at 1 FPS and SKAction.playSoundFileNamed is tied to the scene update rate. Hostia. Replaced the whole thing with AVAudioEngine and a 4-player pool. The kind of fix where you burn an hour confirming the problem is real before spending twenty minutes solving it.

---

Then Apple said no.

Build 23 bounced. Guideline 3.1.2(c) — missing Terms of Use and Privacy Policy links in the subscription flow. The kind of rejection that's completely fair and completely annoying at the same time. You know the rules. You just forgot to follow them because you were busy making explosions look stupid good.

So: legal links on the paywall. Termes d'ús, Política de privacitat. Modal sheets with markdown rendering. Extracted a shared `showLegalSheet()` and consolidated the sheet border radius into a single `AppRadius.sheet` token across three callsites. The work itself took maybe an hour. The context switch from "game feel" to "legal compliance" is the part that costs energy.

Had to actually create the Terms of Use page on the website. Astro, quick markdown file, deploy. While I was in there I found two broken devlog entries — corrupted frontmatter that had been silently 404ing. Fixed those too. Set the custom EULA via `asc eula create`, added the legal section to the App Store description. Three simplify passes to make sure everything was clean.

The glamorous life of indie development: one hour you're tuning particle effects, the next you're writing legal boilerplate in Catalan.

---

Past midnight the mad scientist took over. Rebalanced The Ascent. Reordered nodes. Inheritance moved to slot one. Momentum buffed from +25% to +100% production bonus on ascend. Renamed Swift Return to Purpose. The naming matters more than it should — Purpose implies intent, direction. Swift Return implies running away and coming back. I wanted the node to mean something.

Then The Furnace. Redesigned the whole branch as a milk-centric tapping flywheel. Bellows spawns a magic cookie at 100% milk. White Heat doubles fill rate during frenzy. The loop feeds itself: tap → milk → frenzy → faster fill → more frenzy. It feels like a perpetual motion machine when it's running. It isn't, but the illusion is the game.

Codex caught a ~100ms activation lag — the magic cookie counter only updates at 10Hz, so first taps after Bellows spawn missed the 2x fill. Fixed it with a stored bool that sets eagerly in `activate()`. Zero-cost read on the tap hot path. Avoided the per-tap array scan. The kind of micro-optimization that matters when your game loop runs at 10 FPS and every frame counts.

Renamed The Furnace to The Frenzy. New node names: Kindling, Pummel, Eruption, Overdrive, Inferno. Fire icon progression across the branch. Switched all 25 tech tree node icons from stringly-typed to `ImageResource`. Type safety at 1am feels righteous.

Then the big one. Dropped from 5 branches to 4. Killed The Slumber entirely. Merged its offline theme into The Mint. New flywheel: Night Shift → Appraisal → Overtime → Payroll → Mother Lode. Offline time feeds coins feeds cookies. Removed 10 nodes and their effects across ~10 files. Clean deletion. No migration concerns — the feature hasn't shipped.

That last part is the luxury of unreleased code. You can be brutal with it. No users to break, no data to migrate, no backwards compatibility dance. Just delete and rebuild. It's the closest thing to a blank canvas you get in software that already exists.

After the structural pass, the visual one. Restyled the tech tree detail sheet — duotone background now, dark header bleeding into white content. Bigger title, bigger icon. The locked state used to show a label with the prerequisite name. Killed all that. Just a padlock. If you need to explain that something is locked, your UI already failed.

Converted all 20 effect descriptions from plain strings to AttributedStrings with orange-highlighted keywords — numbers, percentages, CpS, Milk, Magic Cookie, Tentacle. The kind of work that nobody consciously notices and everybody unconsciously feels. First Contact got a proper description for the first time: "A Tentacle latches onto your cookie, slowly draining a portion of your CpS. Tap it 3 times to pop it..." The mechanic deserves words. It was just an icon and a mystery before.

Closed the laptop around 3am. Twelve hours of session. The tech tree went from 5 branches of half-baked ideas to 4 branches with actual flywheel loops. The animation stack went from two competing SpriteKit scenes to one bus with a UIKit fallback. An AB test died and another was born. Apple said no, I said fine, here are your legal links.

Some days you sharpen the knife. Today I used it.
