Easy one today. Or it was supposed to be.

Settings needs a "leave us a review" row. Permanent one — a button that's always there, not the rate-limited `in_app_review` popup that Apple lets you fire three times a year and then just silently ignores. Two different things. The popup is a slot machine. This is a door you can always open.

So I reach for `in_app_review`, obviously. It's right there. It has an `openStoreListing`. Done by lunch.

Except I asked Codex first, mostly out of habit, and it talked me out of it. On Android `openStoreListing` fires a generic `ACTION_VIEW` that can land in a browser, or worse, a chooser dialog — "open with…" and now the user is picking between Chrome and the Play Store to give me five stars they were never going to give me anyway. The `market://` deep link is just more reliable. Direct. iOS gets the `action=write-review` URL that drops you straight on the rating screen, no detour.

So the "easy" library was the wrong call and the slightly-more-manual thing was the right one. I like when that happens. Feels like the universe confirming that the path of least resistance is usually least for a reason.

Skipped the `canLaunchUrl` preflight on purpose. It lies — false negatives all over the place, and to make it tell the truth you have to declare every URL scheme in Info.plist and an Android `<queries>` block, and now I'm maintaining a list of schemes to make a function work that I didn't want in the first place. Just launch it. Catch the failure. Fall back to the https URL. SnackBar if even that dies. Three layers, no manifest surgery. Code quality people would hate it. It works.

The part I actually enjoyed: the icons. The settings rows are on SVGs now — translate, help, star — running through a shared duotone tile. And *that's* where the time went, of course. Not the launch logic, not the fallback chain. The icons. Getting the muted color right, the second tone sitting underneath the first. That's the bit my hands wanted to keep doing. I'd have the whole review flow working and still be nudging the star's fill by a shade nobody will ever consciously notice.

`flutter analyze` is clean. Not device-tested yet, which means it doesn't really work, it just *compiles*. There's a difference and I know it and I'm choosing to feel good anyway.

Good morning of work. Two icons I keep looking at, a button that points people at a review screen for an app almost nobody's downloaded yet. I told myself I'd think about that second part later.

Copied a lightbulb SVG out of the icon pack, dropped it in, relaunched. Black square. Just a solid black square where my white bulb should be. Not the button — the icon itself, a confident opaque rectangle telling me to go to hell.

This is the part the morning's launch-fallback logic never made me feel. The hard stuff I can reason about. It's the *trivially stupid* stuff that breaks me. The bulb was fine. The problem was an invisible full-bleed `<rect>` behind it, hidden by a CSS class — `opacity:0` in a `<style>` block — and the fill colors were CSS too. And flutter_svg just… ignores CSS. So the part that was supposed to be invisible became visible, the part that was supposed to be white became default black, and my icon became a void. The fix was dumb and quick: rewrite the SVG with inline `fill="#ffffff"`, delete the bounding rect. But getting there meant a few kill-and-relaunch cycles because flutter_svg caches the rasterized picture and a hot reload won't touch it, one "Lost connection to device" for flavor, and me squinting at XML wondering when exactly I became a person who debugs other people's stylesheets inside a vector graphic.

Asked ChatGPT for it. Got back four perfectly nice PNGs with the white background baked right in. Opaque. No alpha. Of course.

So instead of begging it to try again I flood-filled the white out myself — PIL, seed from the near-white neutral pixels around the border, threshold 70, and trust the thick dark outlines in the art to stop the fill from bleeding into the cream-colored stuff inside the scene. numpy was there, scipy wasn't, so a hand-rolled flood-fill it was. Cropped to the alpha box. Tried the four variants — bed, no bed, coat rack with the hat — kept the coat rack. Dropped it in behind a `BoxFit.contain` so it scales to any screen without overflowing, and a `Transform.scale(1.1)` to give it ten percent more presence.

This morning I was debugging someone else's CSS inside an SVG. Tonight I'm seeding flood-fills to delete a background an AI flat-out refused to leave out. I genuinely don't know what my job is anymore. I love it.

You don't lay them to rest, you just trip over them on the way to something else.

Then routing, and the one real dead end of the night. I wanted the jump from the case list into a game to go through the orchestrator — the choreographed transition the rest of the app uses — instead of slamming in with a plain opaque page swap. First attempt was, and I'm quoting my own notes, terrible. I made everything transparent so the backgrounds could crossfade. The game background came up black instead of grey. The case envelopes from the screen behind leaked straight through the transparent page like it wasn't there. Because to the transparent page, it wasn't.

The crossfade model only works if *every* covered element gets cleared first — header, difficulty selector, envelopes, all of it. Miss one and it bleeds through. For a screen as dense as the game board that's a fight you lose by default. So I stopped fighting it: keep the game screen fully opaque, exactly the grey it always was, zero leaks, and just fade the whole opaque thing in over the case list while the envelopes slide out underneath it. The envelopes stagger out the bottom, each a hair behind the last, mirroring the way they staggered in. Looks deliberate now. Lesson filed: transparent crossfades are elegant right up until the covering page has a hole in it.

And then the part I'd have stayed up all night for. The board animates in. The room fades up first, then the furniture pops in one piece at a time, staggered, on an elastic curve — *very bouncy*, that was the brief I gave myself, and very bouncy is what I got. Tables and chairs springing into a crime scene. Plays exactly once, the first time you land on a fresh board, then sits perfectly still like nothing ever happened.

It compounds with the page fade, so the first half-second is a little muddy — two animations talking over each other. I sat and watched it a few times trying to decide if that bothered me. It didn't. It looked perfect. Left it.

*This* is the flow. Not the routing logic, not the flood-fills, not the fallback chains. This. The moment furniture bounces onto a screen in exactly the shape it had in my head three minutes ago. I navigated in and out of that board ten times just to watch it land.

Star icon this morning. Badge pill this afternoon. A whole detective office and a board that bounces tonight.

All of it on a home screen nobody's opened, in an app almost nobody's downloaded.
