# March 2, 2026

Morning. The depth button still isn't right.

Yesterday's spring animation was close — the press felt good, the release felt alive — but the bounce on return was landing flat. Like a ball that hits the ground and just... stops. No personality. So I started iterating on the curve.

elasticOut at 120ms. Too tight, too mechanical. Bumped to 200ms — better, more weight to it, but the overshoot felt wrong, like the button was drunk. Then I tried bounceOut at 300ms and something clicked. That little double-tap at the end, the way it settles — it's not a spring anymore, it's a *character*. The button has opinions about how it wants to land.

The drag-out cancellation came together too. If you press the button and drag your finger away before releasing, it cancels the tap. Smooth return to rest, no action fired. It sounds trivial but it's the kind of thing that separates "this feels like a real app" from "this feels like a hackathon demo." The gesture detector needed some convincing — had to track the pointer position relative to the button bounds and decide at what distance to bail. But once it worked, it just felt *correct*. You know it when your thumb knows it.

Then the exam card copy. "Exàmens complets" was bothering me. It's what the database column says, but it's not what a user thinks. They think "exàmens de convocatòries anteriors" — past exam papers. One of those moments where a database schema leaks into the UI and nobody notices because the developer wrote both. Changed it. Small thing. The kind of small thing that matters.

Build 16 to TestFlight. Fumbled the CLI flags on the first attempt — forgot `--export-options-plist` needs the path, not just the flag. Second try went clean. There's always this weird little thrill when the upload completes. The code stops being yours. It's out there now, on someone else's phone, being judged by someone else's thumb. Ship it, watch the bounce.

Afternoon. Different project, different brain.

Switched to the game. Branch 5.4 — production multiplier boost cards. The kind of feature that sounds simple in a sentence and sprawls across six files by the time you're done.

The core idea: spend gems, get a temporary production multiplier. Timer counts down, boost expires, back to normal. Simple enough. But then you need NTP timestamps for anti-cheat because device clocks lie. You need persistence so the boost survives app kills. You need a one-boost-at-a-time rule so they can't stack infinitely. You need the UI to show a countdown with a progress bar that drains smoothly.

Built a new ProductionMultiplierManager — observable singleton, handles expiration, save/load, the works. Wired it into the game state so `getAutomaticProductivity()` picks up the multiplier. Card UI: price button when inactive, countdown progress bar when active. Green fill draining with a minutes-and-seconds timer. Deployed to the physical device. It works.

Then the color iteration spiral.

The progress bar was green. Fine. Functional. Boring. Tried persianGreen — better, but it didn't pop against the card background. Then I color-picked the candy PNG asset and found piggyPinkStrong. Three rounds of tweaking later: candy pink fill draining to reveal caribbeanGreen underneath. No opacity tricks, just one color revealing another as the bar shrinks. The card shadow switches from persianGreen to piggyPinkDark while the boost is active. It's a small thing but the whole card *pulses* differently when a boost is running. You can feel it.

This is the stuff I live for. Not the singleton pattern, not the NTP timestamps. The moment the pink drains and the green peeks through and it looks exactly like the picture in my head.

---

Then a bug report pulled me out of the flow.

Ascension upgrades were producing infinite numbers. Players had found it. Live and exploitable. Joder.

Root cause: three upgrades — Organic Growth, Lean Manufacturing, A Little Maneuver — were using `2^n` with `sqrt(n)` cost scaling. The cost grew slowly but the effect grew exponentially. Classic blowup. A formula I'd written months ago without thinking through the endgame.

Fix: changed all three from `2^n` to `2^sqrt(n)`. Diminishing returns, no overflow. Single file change in Building.swift. The descriptions were vague enough to still fit — "increases production significantly" works whether it's exponential or root-exponential. Pushed it immediately.

Five minutes to fix something that was probably live for days. The gap between "writing the formula" and "understanding the formula at scale" is where bugs like this hide. Past me wrote `2^n` and thought it was fine. Present me wonders what else past me thought was fine.

Two projects, two brains, one day. Ship it, watch the bounce, fix the exploit, move on.
