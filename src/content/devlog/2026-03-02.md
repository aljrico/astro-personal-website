Late night. Big push. The official exam system — eight convocatories with metadata, scoring formula, attempt tracking, the whole thing — went out in one commit. The kind of session where you look up and it's half ten and you've built a complete feature without pausing. Selection screen with places, aspirants, time limits. Results screen with APTE / NO APTE. The scoring formula is real: negative marking, pass at 4.0, the works.

There's something grounding about implementing a formula that thousands of people's careers depend on. `P = (C - I×0.25) / N × 10`. Get it right.

Fifteen minutes later I'm reading my own diff and — hostia. The finish method was mutating state, adding the last answer to the results list, then building the persisted score from that same list. Which now had N+1 elements. Last answer counted twice.

Flutter analyze? Blind to it. The linter doesn't know you just mutated `state` and are now reading derived values from the new version while your brain is still in the old one. Only eyes catch that. Mine almost didn't.

Fixed it in ten minutes. Two lines. The kind of bug that would've shipped and shown scores slightly off — not enough for anyone to notice immediately, just enough to slowly rot trust. The worst kind. Worth tattooing somewhere: in any Notifier method that mutates state and then reads from it, you're holding a loaded gun. Your mental model says "I'm still in the old world." You're not.

Morning. Different energy. Polish mode.

The exam cards had metadata in the wrong order. Questions and time limit should come before places and aspirants. Format info first, context second. Small thing. Obvious once you see it. Invisible until you do.

Needed a separator for the pass ratio. Tried an arrow first. Too dramatic. Middot won. These decisions take thirty seconds and matter more than they should. But this is where the flow lives — the moment the spacing clicks, the weight of bold text against regular, the thing that makes it feel *considered* instead of generated.

Removed the repeat button from results. One button left, so the wrapping Column became noise. Unwrapped it. The kind of cleanup that makes future-me think past-me was tidy. He wasn't. He just got annoyed by unnecessary nesting.

---

Noon. The machine is warm. Two codebases open, switching between them like tuning two instruments.

On the Flutter side, the evaluation screen needed to feel like a real simulacre. No feedback between questions — just answer, advance, answer, advance. Mirrored the pattern from the official exam notifier: isAdvancing guard, 250ms delay, request token to prevent race conditions. Ripped out the per-question feedback, the answer footer, the highlight animations. Net negative twenty-one lines. That's the good kind of commit — the one that deletes more than it adds.

Then the progress bar. The evaluation header had this text-and-pill thing — "Pregunta X de Y" — that felt like a placeholder. Swapped it for the same progress bar widget the other screens use. But here's the part that made me grin: wrapped the fill in a TweenAnimationBuilder. 350ms, easeInOut. One change in the shared widget, and now every screen that uses it — evaluation, simulacre, drill, topics — gets animated progress for free. Four screens. Zero call-site changes. This is the kind of decision that earns itself in the first five seconds.

Over on the iOS side, building the daily rewards card. Horizontal scroll, reward tiles, calendar icon with a streak overlay. The feature itself was straightforward. The color wasn't. Went through caramel, minty, mango, watermelon, some kind of midBlue that looked medical. Settled on a two-tone classicBlue — header and tiles matching, white base. The iteration took longer than the implementation. Always does with visual stuff. That's not wasted time. That's the actual work.

Rounder tile corners. 0.25 felt stiff, 0.32 felt right. Fixed the vertical padding so today's scaled-up tile didn't clip. Matched the shadow to the existing cards. Then a subtitle: "Come back every day for better rewards!" — iterated the opacity from 0.45 to 0.54 in steps of two hundredths. Absolutely unhinged behavior if you describe it out loud. Absolutely necessary if you care about how it feels.

Extended the scroll range so players can scroll all the way back to day one. The auto-scroll to today was already in place, so the UX stays smooth. Then reworked the reward schedule — skins moved from every ten days to every twenty-eight, replaced with fortune wheel spins that scale as the square root of the day. Day 10 gets three spins. Day 100 gets ten. The math felt right. Whether the game design is right, I'll find out when people play it.

---

Back to Flutter. The headers across drill, evaluation, and simulacre were doing the same thing three times. Rule of Three says that's when you earn the abstraction. Extracted `QuestionScreenHeader` into a shared widget. Six files touched, net negative fifty-six lines. Unified the progress bar colors — blue for simulacre, yellow for drill. Topic buttons all got navy, sentence case, icons, Wrap layout. Ran a Codex review on it. Clean.

Then the home screen. The fire logo and title were scrolling with the cards, which always felt wrong. Pinned the header, let only the cards scroll. Simple fix. Would have been simpler if I hadn't started editing topics_screen.dart instead of home_screen.dart. Stared at the diff for a full minute wondering why nothing looked right. Reverted. Applied to the correct file. Venga.

The ESTUDIA button. Bumped the font size, cranked the weight, deepened the shadow. Tried pill-shaped corners and a narrower width. Looked at it. Reverted both. The standard radius was better. Sometimes improving means trying the change, seeing it's worse, and undoing it. The button doesn't know you tried. But you know it earned its shape.

And then I kept staring at the buttons. All of them. ElevatedButton here, FilledButton there, OutlinedButton somewhere else. Six Material buttons across five screens, each doing its own thing. The inconsistency wasn't broken — it was worse than broken. It was *almost* consistent. The kind of thing only you notice, and then you can't un-notice it.

Replaced all six with PressableDepthButton. Depth hierarchy: 6.0 for primary CTAs, 4.0 standard, 3.0 for secondary and outlined variants. The outlined ones get frontBorder plus surface colors. Five screens, one language. Caught a stale English "Retry" that should've been "Reintentar" — the kind of thing that slips through when you think in one language and code in another.

But the real session started after. The button *worked*. It didn't *feel*.

Split the animation in two: 60ms down with easeOutCubic, 120ms up with elasticOut. The asymmetry is everything. Fast compression, bouncy release. Your finger pushes something solid and it springs back like it's alive. Took maybe ten iterations to get the curve right. Each one felt almost identical until it didn't.

Then drag-out cancellation. You press the button, change your mind, slide your finger off — it should spring back and not fire. Obvious behavior. Non-obvious implementation. Went with Listener over GestureDetector because I needed immediate pointer events, no tap disambiguation delay. The tradeoff is manual hit testing — RenderBox, globalToLocal, contains. More code. But the feel is instant. No 100ms ambiguity window where the framework is deciding if you're tapping or dragging. You press, it responds. You leave, it knows.

This is the part that's hard to explain. Nobody will ever think "wow, the drag-out cancellation on this button is really well implemented." But they'd notice if it wasn't there. They'd press, slide away, and the action would fire anyway, and something would feel cheap. The absence of wrongness is the whole job.

The progress bars animate. The home screen stays put. The daily rewards card scrolls back to day one. Every button in the app presses the same way, bounces the same way, cancels the same way.

It's starting to feel like two actual products instead of two prototypes with ambitions. The kind of day where you touch everything and nothing breaks. That's a good place to leave it.
