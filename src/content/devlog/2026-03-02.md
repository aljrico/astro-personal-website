# 2026-03-02

Morning started with the scroll. The home screen header was floating away with the cards — pinned it in place so only the cards move. One of those fixes where you wonder how it ever worked the other way.

Then the ESTUDIA button. Bigger text, bolder weight, more depth. The kind of change that takes five minutes and makes everything around it look like it was always meant to be there. Replaced the last Material buttons with PressableDepthButton while I was at it. Consistency sweep. Satisfying in a way that's hard to explain to non-visual people — like when every picture frame in a room is finally level.

The bounce, though. The bounce was the morning.

Added spring bounce and drag-out cancellation to the depth button. Started with elasticOut at 120ms. Too fast, too robotic. Stretched it to 200ms — better, but still mechanical. Switched to bounceOut at 300ms and there it was. The button had personality. It wanted to come back. It overshot, corrected, settled. Three iterations to get something that feels inevitable in hindsight.

The weird thing about animation work is you're tuning a feeling. There's no unit test for "does this spark joy." You press the button, watch it bounce, and your gut says yes or no. The physicist brain wants to model the spring constant. The artist brain just keeps tapping until it grins.

Afternoon. Fixed the exam card copy — "complets" was showing instead of "de convocatories anteriors." Classic case of a database column name leaking through the UI. The internal label made sense to the schema. Made zero sense to a human reading it on screen. Renamed it and moved on, but it's a good reminder: your data model's vocabulary is not your user's vocabulary. Ever.

Bumped to build 16. Shipped to TestFlight. Fumbled the CLI flags — forgot one, had to re-run. The upload itself is always a strange moment. The code stops being yours. It's out there now, in Apple's pipeline, on its way to someone's phone. There's a small thrill in that handoff, even when "someone" might just be me on another device.

Then I pivoted.

Completely different project. Completely different language. Swift. The idle game. Hadn't touched it in a bit, and the pull was strong — the kind of pull where you're already thinking about the implementation before you've consciously decided to switch.

Built the production multiplier system. New manager class — @Observable singleton, boost expiration tracked via NTP timestamps so you can't cheat by screwing with the device clock. Save/load persistence, cloud sync keys, one-boost-at-a-time rule where activating a new one replaces the old. The card UI snaps between two states: price button when inactive, countdown progress bar when active. Timer ticking down in M:SS format. The whole thing wired into the game state's productivity calculation.

It's a lot of plumbing for something the player sees as "tap button, numbers go brrr." But that's idle games — the iceberg ratio of visible to invisible is absurd.

Then I got lost in color.

The progress bar started green. Obviously green. Then persianGreen because plain green is boring. Then three rounds of iteration later: candy pink. PiggyPinkStrong, specifically — color-picked from the candy asset to match. The bar fills with pink and drains to reveal caribbeanGreen underneath. No opacity tricks, just one color receding to show another.

And the shadow. The card shadow switches from persianGreen to piggyPinkDark while the boost is active. It's subtle. Nobody will consciously notice. But the whole card *glows differently* when the boost is running, and that's the kind of detail that makes a game feel alive instead of functional.

Three hours on two commits. One architectural, one cosmetic. The architectural one took longer but the cosmetic one was more fun. That's always how it goes.

Two projects today. One shipped build, one new system. The Flutter app got its bounce. The idle game got its candy pink. Ship it, watch the bounce, move on.
