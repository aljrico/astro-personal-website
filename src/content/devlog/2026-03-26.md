Two projects in one day. The tower defense got the love. The cookie clicker got the autopsy.

Started the morning ripping out every Material icon in the Flutter game and replacing them with Rhos assets. Cash stacks, crypto coins, gem outlines, battleaxes, hammers. The nav bar went from generic to *themed* in about fifteen minutes. Dropped the text labels too — icon-only, 56px wide. Cleaner. The kind of change where you wonder why you ever had labels in the first place.

Then the upgrade panel. This one took iterations. Two-column grid, no — three-column. Aspect ratio 2.6, no — 1.8, no — 3.0. Button height 36, 50, 54. The kind of pixel-fiddling that looks like indecision from the outside but feels like tuning a radio from the inside. You're turning the dial and you *know* when it clicks. It clicked at three columns, 30% screen width, name on top, cost pill below. Stat values showing the actual number instead of just the increment — base damage is 1.0, not 0.0. That required threading TowerStats through everything, but now there's a single source of truth for stat-by-ID logic. Deleted a duplicate 12-branch switch statement. Felt good.

The polygon envelope for enemy shapes was a nice detour. Triangles that actually look triangular, hexagons that look hexagonal. Modulate base radius with `cos(π/n) / cos(θ mod sector - sector/2)`. The physicist brain got fifteen minutes of joy there. Added random spin per enemy. HP bars stay upright. The little details that nobody notices but everything feels wrong without.

Crit projectiles: tried making them gold. Looked wrong. Made them the same red but 2x size instead. Size communicates better than color when the blob is already pink-red. Design instinct over design theory.

Then the rename. Hostia.

Cash → Coins. Coins → BlueStars. Across sixteen files. Two-phase rename to avoid collisions — coins to blueStar first, then cash to coins. Then BlueStars → Blood across eleven more files. Three renames in one afternoon. The kind of refactor where you hold your breath and hit save and either everything works or everything is on fire. Everything worked. Added backward-compat migration for old save JSON keys. The permanent currency is now "blood," visualized as a red potion drop icon that I had to trim with Pillow because it had 72px of transparent padding on a 256px canvas.

Rebranded the blob colors to match — watermelon pink out, blood-potion scarlet in. Extracted the exact hex from the icon. E62E49. Every downstream visual auto-updates through the shared color refs. Buttons, projectiles, fusion glow, HP gradients. One palette change, twenty things update. That's what you get when you don't hardcode colors. Past me was smart. Or at least consistent.

Font exploration was dangerous. Tried KG Happy Solid — too playful. Barlow Semi Condensed — too corporate. Lilita One — yes. Set it as the global app font, extracted to a constant, removed hardcoded strings. Bundled the rejected fonts anyway because I'll probably change my mind next week.

Tilted checkerboard background. Alternating grays, 56px cells, 10° rotation. Tried 45° first — too much. 10° is the sweet spot where it reads as intentional texture, not a design statement. Cached the Paint objects to avoid per-frame allocation. The performance habit is automatic now.

The whole afternoon was flow. Pure mad scientist. Every change feeding into the next, the game looking more *itself* with each commit. This is the stuff — when you're not building features, you're building identity. The game went from "Flutter template with game mechanics" to something with a visual voice. Lilita One and blood-red blobs and a tilted checkerboard and crossed swords on the nav. It has a personality now.

Then evening. Different project, different mood.

Dug into the LTV drop on the cookie clicker. Full forensic investigation. The Piggy Bank conversion fell off a cliff — 3.1% down to 0.6% — and it happened with v5.0's gem store redesign. Nobody noticed because nobody was looking. It wasn't A/B tested. It just shipped. And the suppressed conversion persisted unchanged across seven subsequent versions. Seven versions. The redesign was the sole cause and it sat there bleeding revenue for months.

That's the kind of finding that makes you feel two things at once. Smart for catching it. Stupid for shipping it without a test in the first place.

Built a new PB card design — bold x2 hero, scattered wings, outlined gem icon — and wired up an A/B test. `boostCardRedesign_v1`. Old versus new. This time we measure.

The day started with making things pretty and ended with learning that making things pretty without measuring can cost you real money. There's a lesson there that I'll probably forget by tomorrow morning when something new looks fun to polish.
