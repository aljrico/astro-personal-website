Started the evening on the Flutter side. Wired up TweenAnimationBuilder on the hero card stats — smooth 800ms integer tweens, a slower thousand-millisecond crawl on the quantile bar, streak count doing its thing. Kept everything StatelessWidget, no controllers, no State classes. Declarative animation the way it should be. Ripped out `_StatColumn`, replaced it with `_AnimatedStatColumn`, dead code gone, analyzer clean. Felt good.

Then discovered go_router recreates HomeScreen on tab navigation. Which means the entry animations replay every time you switch back. Didn't fix it. Noted it. Moved on.

Detour into the Mossos app. Found 39 vague source citations leaked from the LLM generation — things like "segons el text" bolted onto answer options. Meaningless filler. Worse: 22 of them sat on the correct answer. Dead giveaway for anyone paying attention. Test-savvy students would spot the pattern in minutes. Stripped all 39 across four JSONL files, kept the six that actually referenced specific articles. Quick win, big quality bump. The kind of cleanup that's invisible but matters.

Back to the Hollows.

The lore session from earlier kept going. I'd already done three passes on the layer names — landed on geological survey reports, the clinical voice cracking under pressure. But the prologue was still a placeholder. "Your base camp. The light holds here." One line. Said nothing.

Rewrote it as a journal entry for the origin bonfire. The mine is sixty years old but the Hollows are older. Pre-tool. Pre-human. Dynamite sealed the path — forward or nowhere. Spent a good chunk reading back through the lore bible, expedition flavor text, monster lore, the full layer registry. All four expedition pages. Building the connective tissue.

The narrative thesis clicked into place: the Hollows predate the mine. The ore is bait. Excreted by the entity. The mine was built on top of something that *wanted* to be found. That's the spine. Everything else hangs off it.

Then the BigNumber migration. Fangs, coins, ore, attack, defense — all BigNumber now. Twenty-one files, five hundred insertions. The key decision: all currencies must behave as integers even when stored as BigNumber. Added `BigNumber.ceiled` so every multiplication or division by a non-integer gets rounded up. No fractional currency drift. No player sees 2.7 fangs.

Codable changes are backwards-compatible. Existing saves decode through Double fallback paths. Didn't break anyone's game. That's the bar.

Equipment pricing got two passes. First: bone shield drops to fangs-only, daggers normalized to 25 ore each. Second: all shields normalized to 50 ore. Cleaner economy. Less arbitrary.

The scaling work from earlier — `scaledStat(floor:ceiling:progress:)` with ±1/6 variance — still holds. Capped abyssal at ceiling 100 so stats ramp 2000→3500 over depths 45-100. The staircase between tiers still has walls. The hallways between them slope now.

Then I went after the combat narration and that was the most fun hour of the day.

Seven lines rewritten. Killed every fantasy cliché I could find. "Steel finds flesh." Gone. "Blade drinks deep." Gone. "Killing blow lands true." Gone. There was one about "tasting iron and old regret" that made me physically cringe. Too purple. The Dark Souls wipe line got cut too.

The new lines treat the mine as a character. "The mine is tidy like that." "The mine counts each one." The Hollows aren't a backdrop. They're watching. They're keeping score. Kept the candle wipe line because some things earn their melodrama.

And then, because the universe has a sense of timing, halving the character animation delay on the narration text exposed a bug that was always there. The view and the controller both had independent hardcoded delays. At the old speed they masked each other. At the new speed — dead gap. Silence where narration should be. Extracted the character delay as a single source of truth, controller references the view's constant. Fixed. The kind of bug you'd never find unless you changed something adjacent.

Four different projects touched today. Animations, exam cleanup, lore and economy plumbing, combat writing. The writing took the most passes. The BigNumber migration was the most tedious. The combat narration was the most satisfying — there's something about deleting bad prose that feels better than writing good prose. Subtraction as craft.

The lore is getting close to something real. The layers have names that fit. The narration has a voice. The economy scales. None of it's finished but all of it's moving in the same direction, which is more than most days.
