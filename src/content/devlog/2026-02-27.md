Started the evening on the Flutter side. Wired up TweenAnimationBuilder on the hero card stats — smooth 800ms integer tweens, a slower thousand-millisecond crawl on the quantile bar, streak count doing its thing. Kept everything StatelessWidget, no controllers, no State classes. Declarative animation the way it should be. Ripped out `_StatColumn`, replaced it with `_AnimatedStatColumn`, dead code gone, analyzer clean. Felt good.

Then discovered go_router recreates HomeScreen on tab navigation. Which means the entry animations replay every time you switch back. Didn't fix it. Noted it. Moved on.

The Hollows lore was the real session.

I'd been sitting with the layer names for a while and they were wrong. Too literary, too borrowed. Had Eliot quotes, Milton references — the kind of thing that sounds sophisticated in a design doc and falls flat in-game. Nobody's reading T.S. Eliot between combat rounds.

Three passes to find the voice. First attempt was lore-direct: spelled out the mythology, hinted at Anayara, the feeding mine. Too spoilery. Second pass went grandma journals — personal, chatty, first-person accounts from miners. Too warm for what the Hollows are supposed to feel like. Third pass landed: geological survey reports. Clinical language that starts cracking under pressure the deeper you go. The scientist's composure failing. That's the tone.

Renamed everything. The Fungal Shelf became The Shelf. The Altar Depths became The Deep. The Sealed Abyss became The Sealed. Shorter. Heavier. The subtitles do the storytelling now — survey notes that get increasingly unhinged.

Boss names are still generic fantasy monsters. That's next. They should all feel like aspects of one thing, not a bestiary. But not tonight.

Then the scaling problem. Realized that a depth-26 monster and a depth-44 monster were statistically identical. Same HP, same damage. Eighteen floors of nothing changing. That's not difficulty progression, that's a flat line with a costume change.

Built `scaledStat(floor:ceiling:progress:)` with ±1/6 variance. Stats now ramp linearly within each tier while preserving the staircase jumps between tiers — 5x into mid-tier, 5x into serious, 6.7x into abyssal. The walls are still walls. But the hallways between them actually slope upward now.

Capped the abyssal tier at ceiling 100 so stats ramp 2000→3500 over depths 45-100 instead of sitting flat. Discussed the boss formula — depth-100 monster times a layer multiplier times a config value that's currently dead code. Didn't wire it. The formula's right but the plumbing isn't there yet.

---

Afternoon. Switched projects entirely. The exam prep app had LLM artifacts rotting in the question bank — 39 vague citations across four data files. "Segons el text," "com indica el document" — the kind of non-source a language model invents when it's trying to sound authoritative. Twenty-two of them parked on the correct answer. Might as well underline it in red. Any student paying attention to the pattern gets free points. Stripped them all, kept six that referenced actual legal articles. Pushed it. Small fix, real quality win.

Back to the Hollows. Big migration: every currency in the game — fangs, coins, ore, attack, defense — moved to BigNumber. Twenty-one files, five hundred insertions. The design constraint that made it interesting: all currencies must behave as integers even when stored as BigNumber. Built `BigNumber.ceiled` to enforce it — every multiplication or division by a non-integer multiplier gets ceiled afterward so you never see 3.7 fangs. Fractional currency drift is the kind of bug that doesn't show up in testing and ruins someone's save file six months later.

Made the Codable changes backwards-compatible. Double fallback paths so existing deployed saves decode without breaking. The boring kind of engineering that matters more than the fun kind.

Equipment pricing got two passes. First: bone shield drops its red ore cost, goes fangs-only. Daggers normalized to 25 ore across sapphire, golden, obsidian. Second pass: all shields to 50 ore. Clean numbers. The economy is starting to feel like it has a shape instead of a spreadsheet.

Then more lore. Wrote a prologue journal entry for the origin bonfire — replaced the placeholder one-liner that's been sitting there since the beginning. The mine is sixty years old but the Hollows are older. Pre-tool. Pre-human. Dynamite sealed the path. Forward or nowhere.

Spent time reading the full lore bible, expedition flavor text, monster descriptions, layer registry. All of it. Arrived at the narrative thesis I'd been circling: the Hollows predate the mine. The ore is bait — excreted by whatever lives down there. The mine was built on top of something that wanted to be found. That's the sentence the whole game hangs on. Took all day to get there but now it's load-bearing.

Combat narration next. Went through the lines in the combat sheet and killed every fantasy cliché I could find. "Steel finds flesh." Gone. "Blade drinks deep." Gone. "Killing blow lands true." Gone. The silence-nothing repeated endings, the "tasting iron and old regret" — too purple, too borrowed, too Dark Souls. The new lines treat the mine as a character. "The mine is tidy like that." "The mine counts each one." Grittier. More sensory. The mine watches. Kept the candle wipe line. That one earned its place.

Then a timing bug surfaced. Combat narration and the flow controller had independent hardcoded character delays — a pre-existing desync that only became visible after speeding up the text. The view said 0.03, the controller was still waiting at 0.06. Extracted the delay as a single source of truth. The kind of fix that takes five minutes once you see it and could hide for years if you don't.

Five kinds of work today. Animations, prose, math, systems architecture, data cleanup. Two different projects. The lore thesis was the most important — the Hollows have a reason to exist now, not just a map. The combat narration was the most satisfying. Cutting bad writing is its own flow state. The BigNumber migration was the most responsible. And the exam cleanup was the most quietly useful thing nobody will ever notice.

Some days you touch everything and nothing's finished. But the mine has a voice now. That counts.
