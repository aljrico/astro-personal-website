Started the afternoon with housekeeping. Rewrote CLAUDE.md and SPEC.md to match reality — purged every last Expo and TypeScript reference, replaced them with the Flutter stack that's actually running. 637 insertions, 732 deletions. Net negative lines. The old TS examples were verbose. The Dart ones say the same thing in less space. There's a metaphor in there somewhere but I'm not reaching for it.

Then the fun started.

The Mossos d'Esquadra exam needs law texts, and the Catalan government publishes them through Portal Jurídic. Which has an API. Which is undocumented. Which I cracked anyway. POST to a DOGC endpoint with a document ID, get back structured JSON with the full legal text in a nested hierarchy. Hostia, it felt like picking a lock. Five laws downloaded, plus one from the BOE, 397 articles, 650K of markdown. Had to shell out to curl because Python's requests library chokes on their ancient TLS handshake. The kind of workaround you'd be embarrassed about if anyone saw it, but it works and nobody's watching.

Wrong document ID for the Codi d'ètica. 643826 instead of 685656. The kind of bug that takes an hour to find and one second to fix.

The rest was infrastructure. Made the chunker and question generator exam-agnostic — added `--source-dir` flags, dynamic path resolution, exam names injected into prompts. The kind of work that feels like sharpening knives instead of cutting. But this time it was necessary. You can't run a second exam through a pipeline that has "bombers" hardcoded everywhere.

The chunker ate the Mossos laws and spat out 643 chunks. Except the Codi d'ètica, which produced zero. It's structured around prose sections, not numbered articles, and my chunker only knows articles. Parked it. A problem for another day.

Submitted the full batch to OpenAI. 620 tasks. Estimated cost: $1.66. There's something absurd about generating an entire exam's worth of content for less than a coffee.

Results came back. 1,882 questions from 620 out of 620 tasks. Zero failures. The number looks great on paper. Then you look closer.

51.6% of correct answers at index 0. Position bias. The model is lazy — it puts the right answer first and builds distractors around it. 29 questions with malformed options — empty strings, duplicates. And zero D5 questions. I asked for comprehension-level items and got 1,882 memory-recall flashcards. The model did what I told it to do, not what I wanted it to do. My fault, not its.

Filtered down to 1,863 clean questions. Committed, pushed. It's a first batch. It's not good enough yet. The prompt needs explicit shuffle instructions, length-rank compliance checks, a separate D5 generation pass.

The pattern keeps repeating: getting the machine to produce output is easy. Getting it to produce *good* output is the actual work. And the gap between the two is where all the interesting problems live.

1,863 questions. Not one of them ready for a student who's betting their career on passing. We'll get there.

Morning after. Different energy. Yesterday was exploration — cracking APIs, watching batch jobs run, the dopamine of big numbers appearing. Today is plumbing.

Wired the Mossos exam into the app. Fifteen files touched. Routes now carry `examId` everywhere — `/topics/:examId`, `/drill/:examId/:topicId`, the whole tree. The DB builder gained an `--add-exam` flag so exams stack instead of overwriting. 24 topics total now. 19 Bombers, 5 Mossos. 10,762 questions in one SQLite file. The numbers are satisfying even if half of them need work.

Of course it didn't just slot in. The Mossos chunk IDs are hashes. The question chunk IDs are heading paths. They don't match. Yesterday's clean pipeline, today's impedance mismatch. Built a fallback that derives topics directly from the question `topic_id`s instead of requiring chunk alignment. It's duct tape, but it's duct tape that holds.

Tried `FamilyAsyncNotifier` from Riverpod to parameterize providers by exam — the "proper" approach. Doesn't exist without codegen. Pivoted to `FutureProvider.family` in about two minutes. Simpler. Worked immediately. The right abstraction is the one that exists.

Then the bug. Ran a Mossos evaluation on the simulator and got Bombers questions back. The evaluation queries weren't scoped by exam. Of course they weren't — the whole app was single-exam until yesterday. Introduced Dart 3 record types as composite provider keys — `TopicKey`, `EvaluationQuestionCountKey` — instead of bare strings. Type-safe, no more cross-exam leaks. The kind of bug that's trivial to fix once you see it and invisible until you do.

Spent the last stretch doing a self-code-review. The DB builder was reading each JSONL file twice — once for validation, once for insertion. Extracted a pure `parse_question_file()` that does one pass: read, validate, parse, done. Insert loop just writes pre-validated data. The question generator had a closure mutating variables from the enclosing scope. Killed it. Plain list accumulator, post-loop dedup. No behavior changes. Just the physicist brain cleaning up after the mad scientist.

Three commits in ninety minutes. The kind of session where nothing is dramatic and everything is necessary. Yesterday I cracked a lock. Today I installed the door.

Then I went back for the questions the model couldn't write.

LO 2/1986, the law that created the Mossos corps. Only had 4 L4 analysis questions — trap-style items where you need to know not just the rule but the exception, the edge case, the precise wording that trips you up. The batch API produced zero of these. So I sat down with the raw chunks and wrote 30 by hand. Well — Claude wrote them while I directed, which is the same thing and also not the same thing at all. Covered all five títols. Set `chunk_id` to null because the article-to-chunk hash mapping doesn't exist yet. Another seam showing.

Rebuilt the pipeline end to end. 10,792 questions now. Thirty more than an hour ago. The number barely moved. But those 30 are the kind that make a student pause, reread, second-guess themselves — which is exactly what the exam does. Quantity is easy. Precision is the work.

Cleaned up the mess. The DB builder got a proper refactor — writes to a temp file now, streams inserts per exam, so a failure mid-build doesn't corrupt the production database. Should have done this from the start. The kind of thing you only think about after you've been burned, or after you've had enough coffee to imagine being burned. Bumped the build number, committed the macOS CocoaPods integration that had been sitting dirty for days, regenerated the Bombers drill topics with fresh L5 counts. Three commits, working tree clean. The satisfaction of `nothing to commit` after a messy morning.
