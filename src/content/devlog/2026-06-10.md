The old hint button reveals a cell. You're stuck, you tap it, the game hands you an answer. Effective and joyless. It doesn't make the puzzle easier — it makes it shorter, which is a different thing, and a worse one.

So today: global clues. Board-level hints that are *true* and *redundant*. "Nobody was murdered in the kitchen." "No one stood beside the candlestick." Things the story would plausibly say, that narrow the space without pointing at the solution. You read it, you feel smarter, the puzzle stays a puzzle.

Here's the part that made me grin. How do you guarantee a hint actually helps? Not "feels like it helps" — *helps*. You add the redundant constraint, you re-run the solver, you measure the difficulty tier dropping. The drop is the help. It's a number. You can't fake it, you can't over-shoot it, you can dial difficulty *down* by a known amount. The physicist brain lit up like a slot machine. Measurable help. Joder, that's clean.

Phase 1 was all foundation. A `GlobalClue` abstraction — holds, applyTo, describe, serialize — and five concrete types. The solver learned to take extra clues for free, no tier penalty, invariant-guarded so a clue can never quietly touch the singleton or victim domains and corrupt a solution. The whole thing defaults to an empty list, which means every existing puzzle is byte-identical to yesterday. Sixty-seven tests green. The shipped game didn't move a pixel.

That last bit is the quiet pride of the day. I built an entire engine and the player-facing game is *exactly the same*. No regressions because there's nothing to regress yet. Foundation work has this strange texture — you do a lot and produce, visibly, nothing. The dopamine is all internal. Nobody gets to see the elegant invariant guard. It just sits there, correct.

Review passed. No soundness bugs, which for a constraint solver is the only thing that matters — a hint that's subtly false would be worse than no hint at all. Folded in two test fixes and left myself a breadcrumb for future-me: the Phase 2 generator *must* assert `holds(solution)` at emission time, because the runtime solver runs with the invariant check off. Easy to forget. Future-me will thank present-me or curse him, no in-between.

Future-me showed up at lunch.

I'd told myself the generator was tomorrow's problem — the actually-hard part, sleep on it. Instead I just kept going. Enumerate the true candidate clues, filter out the trivial and the over-powered, score them on how much they help, pick five ordered gentle-to-strong. Behind a config flag, default off, so the engine tests stay fast and the shipped game still doesn't move. The app opts in later.

And then I lost an hour to the scoring metric, which is the part worth writing down because I did it backwards. The morning's whole pitch was "measure the tier drop" — so naturally I started there. Too lumpy. A tier is a coarse bucket; you get a third to two-thirds of a level per clue, and easy puzzles already sit at the bottom of the scale, so a clue that genuinely helps an easy board scored *zero*. Tried a cheaper fixpoint solve. Same dead spot at the bottom. Then the obvious thing I should have led with: just count how many candidate cells the clue directly eliminates from the raw base domains. Around 4.4 per level, full coverage, every tier. The clever metrics were me being clever. The simple one was right. There's a lesson in there I'll forget by next week.

One call to make: `RoomCardinality`. It's relational — it doesn't rule out any *specific* cell, so it can't be scored on the elimination axis at all. Deferred it. The type stays, built and tested, parked until it earns its own scoring. Four shipped types, all rule-out: nobody-was-the-murderer, empty-room, no-one-beside-the-prop, no-one-on-the-floor.

Review caught a good one. Greedy picks the strongest marginal clues descending, but you *reveal* them ascending, gentle-to-strong — so a clue that's a dud in reveal order could still sneak into the list. Fixed with a final reveal-order pass. Two smaller things too, and the test now walks the reveal prefix asserting each clue strictly narrows beyond the ones before it. Seventy green.

Phase 3: wire it on, re-bake the campaign. Flip authoring to on, bump two versions, regenerate all 18 levels. Purely additive — every placement, difficulty, seed byte-identical, only the clue list appended. Same quiet trick as the morning: I changed everything and the game didn't move. All 18 levels now carry three to five clues.

Then the hostia. The four "X wasn't the murderer" clues were hogging the picks — and *leaking*. Exonerate four suspects by elimination and you've just named the killer. The over-power guard blocks any single clue from solving the puzzle, but it never sees four of them ganging up. Capped it at two per type. Variety back, at least two suspects left un-exonerated, killer safe. Review passed clean after that — nothing required, on-device authoring runs once on the accepted puzzle, basically free.

One thing I'm carrying to Phase 4 and deliberately not solving: that same guard only blocks a *single* clue. Buy *all* of a level's clues and the board can still hand itself over. The MVP answer is almost cynically convenient — let every clue be revealable, ad-gated, so the player still does the placement and I get more ad impressions for the privilege. Pricing and caps are a later-me problem. Noted, not blocking.

Three commits stacked on master now. Still haven't pushed. The thing has an engine, a generator, and a baked campaign full of true redundant clues — and the remote still thinks it's yesterday. Phase 4 is where it stops being a number and starts being something you can tap: swap the hint button to reveal the next clue instead of a cell, build the Case Notes sheet, count and persist per level, localize the four types in two languages, handle the zero-clue case gracefully. That's the part the player actually sees. Everything today was the part only I get to.
