The hint button always bugged me. The old one just revealed a cell — handed you the answer, killed the puzzle. Cheap.

Today I started replacing it with something I actually like: global clues. Board-level facts that are *true* and fit the fiction — "the victim wasn't the murderer," "this room was empty," "no one stood beside the candlestick." Diegetic. They read like a detective's notes, not like the game giving up.

The killer angle — and this is the part I kept grinning about — is that the help is *measurable*. Add a redundant true constraint, re-solve the board, watch the difficulty tier drop. You can literally quantify how much easier you made it without ever touching the answer. Tune it gentler by exactly this much. Joder, that's clean. That's the physicist brain getting exactly what it wants for once.

Phase 1 today: just the engine. A GlobalClue abstraction — five types, each one knows how to hold, apply itself, describe itself. The solver learned to swallow extra clues for free, no difficulty penalty, invariant-guarded so a clue can never reach into the answer's domain. Existing puzzles come out byte-identical — defaulted the new field to an empty list, so nothing that ships today changed at all. 67 tests green. Review passed, no soundness bugs, which on a solver change is the part that actually scares me.

And that's it. That's the whole day. No player will ever see a line of it.

Because this is foundation. The thing that makes a hint *feel* good — the Case Notes panel sliding up, the reveal, the little count of clues you've spent — none of that exists yet. Three phases away. Today was plumbing. Good plumbing, the kind where the abstraction clicks and every test goes green and you sit back satisfied. But plumbing. No flow state. Flow lives in the visual, and there was nothing to look at.

Left a breadcrumb for tomorrow-me: the Phase 2 generator has to assert each clue holds against the real solution *before* it emits, because the runtime solver runs with the invariant switched off. Easy place to ship a lie. Future me, don't skip it.

Next is the generator — enumerate every true thing about a board, throw out the trivial ones and the over-powered ones, score what's left by how marginally it helps, pick five and order them gentle to strong. That's the fun part. That's where it starts to look like a feature instead of an interface.

But not today. Today I built the part nobody claps for.
