The old hint button reveals a cell. You're stuck, you tap it, the game hands you an answer. Effective and joyless. It doesn't make the puzzle easier — it makes it shorter, which is a different thing, and a worse one.

So today: global clues. Board-level hints that are *true* and *redundant*. "Nobody was murdered in the kitchen." "No one stood beside the candlestick." Things the story would plausibly say, that narrow the space without pointing at the solution. You read it, you feel smarter, the puzzle stays a puzzle.

Here's the part that made me grin. How do you guarantee a hint actually helps? Not "feels like it helps" — *helps*. You add the redundant constraint, you re-run the solver, you measure the difficulty tier dropping. The drop is the help. It's a number. You can't fake it, you can't over-shoot it, you can dial difficulty *down* by a known amount. The physicist brain lit up like a slot machine. Measurable help. Joder, that's clean.

Phase 1 was all foundation. A `GlobalClue` abstraction — holds, applyTo, describe, serialize — and five concrete types. The solver learned to take extra clues for free, no tier penalty, invariant-guarded so a clue can never quietly touch the singleton or victim domains and corrupt a solution. The whole thing defaults to an empty list, which means every existing puzzle is byte-identical to yesterday. Sixty-seven tests green. The shipped game didn't move a pixel.

That last bit is the quiet pride of the day. I built an entire engine and the player-facing game is *exactly the same*. No regressions because there's nothing to regress yet. Foundation work has this strange texture — you do a lot and produce, visibly, nothing. The dopamine is all internal. Nobody gets to see the elegant invariant guard. It just sits there, correct.

Review passed. No soundness bugs, which for a constraint solver is the only thing that matters — a hint that's subtly false would be worse than no hint at all. Folded in two test fixes and left myself a breadcrumb for future-me: the Phase 2 generator *must* assert `holds(solution)` at emission time, because the runtime solver runs with the invariant check off. Easy to forget. Future-me will thank present-me or curse him, no in-between.

Next is the generator — the actually-hard part. Enumerate true candidate clues, filter out the trivial and the over-powered, score them on marginal difficulty drop, pick five ordered gentle-to-strong. Then re-bake the puzzles, then the app: swap the button, build the Case Notes modal. The plan's all there in my head and half on disk.

Except it wasn't next. It was now. By early afternoon the generator existed, and so did the re-baked campaign sitting on top of it.

And the scoring is where the morning's confidence met reality. Remember the tier drop being "the number"? Turns out it was the wrong number. Integer tier-drop is too lumpy — most clues move a puzzle three- or six-tenths of a tier and the rounding eats it, so half my candidates scored zero and looked useless when they weren't. Tried a cheap-fixpoint measure instead. Worse: any level already solvable at tier four scores zero because the solver never breaks a sweat, so every easy puzzle reported "no clue helps." Two metrics, both confidently wrong.

The thing that actually worked was dumber and better: count how many candidate cells a clue directly eliminates from the raw domains. No solver tiers, no rounding cliffs. This clue rules out four placements, that one rules out one. About four and a half clues scored per level, every tier covered, nothing flagged useless that wasn't. The *idea* of measurable help survived. The measurement I was so proud of this morning didn't. I just had to stop being clever about it.

One casualty. There's a clue type — room cardinality, "exactly N people in this room" — that's relational. It doesn't rule out any specific cell, so it can't be scored on the elimination axis at all. Built it, tested it, and then set it aside. It's sitting there fully working, defended by its own tests, doing nothing, waiting for a scoring model that fits it. Shipped the four rule-out types instead and told myself I'd re-add it later with its own math. "Later" is doing a lot of work in that sentence and I know it.

Then a gotcha that made me laugh out loud. Four of the clue types are flavours of "X wasn't the murderer." Generate enough of those and the player doesn't need the puzzle — cross off enough suspects and the last one standing is the killer, handed over for free by pure elimination. The engine was quietly solving its own mystery. Added a per-type cap: max two of any kind. Keeps the variety, leaves at least a couple of suspects un-exonerated, plugs the leak.

Re-baked all eighteen campaign levels. The satisfying part is the diff: purely additive. Every placement, every difficulty, every seed byte-identical to before — the only thing that changed is a list of clues appended to each case. Same puzzles. They just carry three to five hints now. The version bumps are bookkeeping; the actual play didn't shift.

Two more reviews, both Opus at full tilt. Phase 2 folded three fixes, the ugliest a real bug — exactly the kind of thing the morning's breadcrumb was sniffing at. I scored and validated candidates strongest-first but reveal them gentlest-first, so a clue that looked fine in score-order could be a dud in the order the player actually sees it. Fixed with a final pass that re-checks marginal helpfulness in reveal-order. Phase 3 came back clean. Nothing to change.

Then the app. And here's the joke: I'd filed it under tomorrow, and tomorrow showed up early again. Phase 4 by mid-afternoon. First thing I did was delete the old hint — the cell-revealer I opened this morning complaining about, finally in the bin where it belongs. In its place, a Case Notes sheet: a modal that lists the clues you've uncovered and an ad-gated button to uncover the next one. Plumbing modeled on the solved-cases store, clues persisted to disk so they survive a restart. I even got to be petty about the copy — the empty-room clue had to be phrased "No one was in the kitchen," because castellano makes you agree gender with the room and I wasn't about to write four variants of the same sentence. Reviewed by Opus, folded three fixes, feature complete. Four phases in one day.

Then I actually played it. Loaded the thing up and ran a real game, like a player, not a builder. And the modal was wrong. Not broken — wrong. You tap the hint, a sheet slides up, you tap *again* to reveal, you read, you dismiss. Two taps and a context switch for one line of text you already paid an ad for. The friction was the whole experience. So I killed it. Hours old, reviewed, shipped that same afternoon — deleted. The hint button reveals the next clue directly now, no sheet, and the clues stack above the board as little caribbean-green pills. The modal died but the plumbing under it lived — the store, the persistence, the ad gate all survived into the pills. Only the wrapper was wrong.

Getting those pills to sit right is the part of the day my hands actually enjoyed. Hot reload open, nudging padding and alignment until they snapped into place above the board like they'd always been there. That's the flow I chase — not the solver, not the scoring math, the moment the screen finally looks like the picture in my head. Built and killed in an afternoon and I don't regret either half. The modal had to exist for me to see it was wrong. Different from the room-cardinality clue still in cold storage upstairs — that one's *postponed*, waiting for math that fits it. The modal isn't postponed. It's gone. And honestly that's the cleaner fate of the two.

One thing I'm carrying forward and not pretending I've solved: the over-power guard only stops a *single* clue from cracking a puzzle. It says nothing about buying all of them. Reveal every clue on a thin level and you can absolutely get walked to the answer. For the MVP I'm leaving them all revealable — they're ad-gated, so more reveals just means more ads, and the player still does the placement. The candidate fix is a cap at one-less-than-the-clue-count, so there's always a last deduction left for the player to make. Pricing and caps are a problem for the version that has players. Flagging it, not blocking on it.

Still not pushed. The stack's taller now — foundation, generator, re-bake, the app, the pill rewrite — and somewhere in the middle the branch quietly went from master to main. All of it local. The whole feature exists end to end, engine to pills, and the only computer that knows is this one. The work is real before anyone can see it, which is the opposite of how I usually need things to be. Yesterday's plan said the player would finally meet this thing. They still haven't. Tomorrow it gets pushed, or it doesn't — and the gap between "done" and "shipped" goes on being the part I'm worst at.
