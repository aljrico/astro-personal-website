Split day. Two projects, two completely different headspaces. Three rounds.

Started with the FlamaCoach pipeline — the "current affairs" module for the Catalan police exam. 18,000 headlines from 3cat, filter them down to what's actually exam-worthy, generate questions. The kind of work that feels like plumbing until the numbers start making sense. Raised the worthiness threshold from 6 to 8 and suddenly 809 articles survived instead of thousands of noise. That ratio felt right. You know it when you see it.

The categorization was a mess though. Nineteen upstream categories splattering into topics that nobody could study from. So I built a grouping mechanism — merge the noise into eight clean thematic buckets. Internacional, Política Espanyola, Catalunya, all the way through Economia i Ciència. Went from chaos to something a student could actually navigate. There's a particular satisfaction in taking messy real-world data and imposing just enough structure that it becomes useful without being dishonest. Stupid for not catching it earlier. Smart for tracing it back to the root.

64 topics. 12,741 questions. Rebuilt the DB, pushed it. Done.

Then the cookie clicker called.

A wealth bug after ascension. Players ascending and keeping their pre-ascension production for one tick — enough to leak wealth that should have been zeroed. Race condition. `BuildingsManager.reset()` was clearing everything except the cached `buildingsProduction` stored property, so when the game unpaused, the first tick multiplied stale production by delta time and handed the player free cookies. The fix was trivially small — zero the cache in `reset()`, belt-and-suspenders the productivity in both ascension paths. The finding was the hard part. Tracing the full ascension flow, understanding why `unPause()` was winning the race against `reset()`. A research agent actually spotted it, which is both convenient and slightly humbling. The machine found what I missed.

Same bug existed in the transcendence path. Of course it did.

Code review of the Hollows commits after that. Six commits, four flags, one actual fix. The typewriter animation in the chest sheet could spawn orphaned Tasks if `.onAppear` fired multiple times — cancel the previous task before starting a new one. One line. The kind of bug that only manifests when someone swipes back and forth fast enough, which means it would have found the one user who does that and driven them insane.

Afternoon shift: SpriteKit. Six SpriteView instances living in a ZStack, all rendering Metal pipelines whether anything's happening on screen or not. The idea was simple — track which effect scenes are actually active, unmount the idle ones. Built a little `@Observable` singleton to track active state per scene, conditionally swap `SpriteView` for `Color.clear` when nothing's playing.

First attempt broke the layout immediately. Pulling a SpriteView out of the ZStack collapsed it because `.frame()` and `.edgesIgnoringSafeArea` were attached to the SpriteView itself. Remove the view, lose the frame, watch everything shift. The fix was wrapping each one so the frame modifiers stay applied regardless of what's inside. Swap the contents, keep the container. One of those bugs where the solution is obvious in hindsight but the symptom is confusing as hell — "why is my entire background gone?"

Results: GPU submissions dropped 36%. From 15k to 9.6k per second. CPU barely moved — 19% to 18%. Which tells the real story. SpriteKit was only eating 3% of the main thread. The actual hog is SwiftUI's async renderer. Good to know where the ceiling is, even if you can't punch through it yet.

Then a code review of the optimization itself caught three real bugs I'd shipped. Scene animations running against a nil view when the SpriteView gets unmounted. Stale actions carrying over on remount because `didMove(to:)` wasn't pausing active bursts. And PermanentBoost had no ref-counting, so multiple enable/disable cycles leaked. All three legitimate. All three fixed and pushed. The kind of review that makes you grateful someone else looked at it, even if "someone" is a machine. Especially because it's a machine. No ego, no politics, just "hey, this will crash."

But the 36% wasn't enough. Two always-on SpriteViews still remained. MainScene was the most offensive — a transparent SKScene with zero visual content whose only job was forwarding SpriteKit's 60 FPS `update(_:)` callback to the game loop. An entire Metal render pipeline — transparent alpha compositing at 60 Hz — just to get a timer callback. Like hiring a symphony orchestra to play your alarm clock.

Ripped it out. Replaced it with a plain `CADisplayLink`. Deleted MainScene.swift, deleted MainSceneView.swift. The game loop doesn't care where its heartbeat comes from — CADisplayLink fires the same delta, the accumulator normalizes it to the same 10 ticks/second. SpriteKit render samples dropped 76%. The remaining samples are MainJuiceScene doing actual audio work. Everything else is gone.

Tried to get proper GPU traces on the physical device with xctrace's Game Performance template. First two captures caught app startup instead of idle state. Then xctrace locked up entirely — "An unknown problem is preventing this device from recording." Survived device reboot, daemon restarts, everything. Some Metal instrumentation cleanup bug that just refuses to let go. Fell back to `sample` on an iPhone SE simulator. Not ideal, but the stack data told the story clearly enough.

Shipped v5.3 build 3 to TestFlight. 53 commits since the marketing version bump. Cut a new branch for 5.4. Also dialed back the opt-in ad gem reward probability from 25% to 10% — the economy was running too hot.

Then FlamaCoach pulled me back in for the evening round.

Home screen polish. The kind of session where you're not building features, you're making the thing *feel* right. Renamed "Simulacres" to "Exàmens Oficials" because that's what they are and nobody was going to guess what "Simulacres" meant without context. Restyled the CTA buttons — "ESTUDIA" in a muted crimson, "OBRIR EXÀMENS" in a lighter blue. Tried yellow for ESTUDIA first. Reverted within minutes. Some colors look great in your head and terrible on a navy gradient. The blue needed to be lighter than the card background to read as a distinct interactive element — `#4A6FA8` against the darker gradient. Small thing. The kind of thing that takes ten minutes to figure out and makes you wonder why design systems exist and also why they never quite work.

Details nobody notices. Details everybody feels.

The most iterative part was the motivational quotes. Thirty-five hand-curated Catalan phrases for streaks of three or more, selected deterministically by day-of-year so you never get the same one twice in a row. Sounds simple. Ten rounds of back and forth. Rejecting gendered language — no "campió/campiona." Rejecting overpromising tones — no "el teu esforç donarà fruits" because who am I to promise that. Rejecting proverb-style because it reads as patronizing from an app. Rejecting generic filler because generic filler is worse than nothing. What survived is punchy modern Catalan. Direct.
