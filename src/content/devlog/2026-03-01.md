Split day. Two projects, two completely different headspaces.

Started with the FlamaCoach pipeline — the "current affairs" module for the Catalan police exam. 18,000 headlines from 3cat, filter them down to what's actually exam-worthy, generate questions. The kind of work that feels like plumbing until the numbers start making sense. Raised the worthiness threshold from 6 to 8 and suddenly 809 articles survived instead of thousands of noise. That ratio felt right. You know it when you see it.

The categorization was a mess though. Nineteen upstream categories splattering into topics that nobody could study from. So I built a grouping mechanism — merge the noise into eight clean thematic buckets. Internacional, Política Espanyola, Catalunya, all the way through Economia i Ciència. Went from chaos to something a student could actually navigate. There's a particular satisfaction in taking messy real-world data and imposing just enough structure that it becomes useful without being dishonest. The "Uncategorized" bloc turning out to be Àmbit C because the drill-topics were generated without proper bloc definitions — that's the kind of bug that makes you feel stupid and smart at the same time. Stupid for not catching it earlier. Smart for tracing it back to the root.

64 topics. 12,741 questions. Rebuilt the DB, pushed it. Done.

Then the cookie clicker called.

A wealth bug after ascension. Players ascending and keeping their pre-ascension production for one tick — enough to leak wealth that should have been zeroed. Race condition. `BuildingsManager.reset()` was clearing everything except the cached `buildingsProduction` stored property, so when the game unpaused, the first tick multiplied stale production by delta time and handed the player free cookies. The fix was trivially small — zero the cache in `reset()`, belt-and-suspenders the productivity in both ascension paths. The finding was the hard part. Tracing the full ascension flow, understanding why `unPause()` was winning the race against `reset()`. A research agent actually spotted it, which is both convenient and slightly humbling. The machine found what I missed.

Same bug existed in the transcendence path. Of course it did.

Code review of the Hollows commits after that. Six commits, four flags, one actual fix. The typewriter animation in the chest sheet could spawn orphaned Tasks if `.onAppear` fired multiple times — cancel the previous task before starting a new one. One line. The kind of bug that only manifests when someone swipes back and forth fast enough, which means it would have found the one user who does that and driven them insane.

Afternoon shift: SpriteKit. Six SpriteView instances living in a ZStack, all rendering Metal pipelines whether anything's happening on screen or not. The idea was simple — track which effect scenes are actually active, unmount the idle ones. Built a little `@Observable` singleton to track active state per scene, conditionally swap `SpriteView` for `Color.clear` when nothing's playing.

First attempt broke the layout immediately. Pulling a SpriteView out of the ZStack collapsed it because `.frame()` and `.edgesIgnoringSafeArea` were attached to the SpriteView itself. Remove the view, lose the frame, watch everything shift. The fix was wrapping each one so the frame modifiers stay applied regardless of what's inside. Swap the contents, keep the container. One of those bugs where the solution is obvious in hindsight but the symptom is confusing as hell — "why is my entire background gone?"

Results: GPU submissions dropped 36%. From 15k to 9.6k per second. CPU barely moved — 19% to 18%. Which tells the real story. SpriteKit was only eating 3% of the main thread. The actual hog is SwiftUI's async renderer. Good to know where the ceiling is, even if you can't punch through it yet.

Then a code review of the optimization itself caught three real bugs I'd shipped. Scene animations running against a nil view when the SpriteView gets unmounted. Stale actions carrying over on remount because `didMove(to:)` wasn't pausing active bursts. And PermanentBoost had no ref-counting, so multiple enable/disable cycles leaked. All three legitimate. All three fixed and pushed. The kind of review that makes you grateful someone else looked at it, even if "someone" is a machine. Especially because it's a machine. No ego, no politics, just "hey, this will crash."

Ended the day back in FlamaCoach removing a placeholder string that said "Contingut pendent de descripció" on topics with no description. Just hide the widget entirely. Small things. The kind of change that takes thirty seconds and makes the app feel less like a prototype.

March. Three months into the year. Two apps in motion, neither finished, both alive. The pipeline hums, the clicker clicks, the GPU submits 36% less nonsense. Venga.
