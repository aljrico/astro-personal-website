Apple bounced 5.18. Guideline 3.1.1 again — permanent purchases with no visible way to restore them. Their suggested fix was account registration. No. I'm not bolting a login system onto a cookie clicker because a reviewer wants a restore path. The purchases already live in iCloud; the fix is a button that says so.

So this morning: a Restore Purchases button in Settings. House card style, tap it and it spins for at least a second even if the work finishes faster — because a button that responds instantly looks like it did nothing. Spinner, checkmark, done. Under the hood it kicks the App Store sync, refetches transactions, pulls cloud storage. Registered the file in pbxproj by hand like a caveman. Pushed as 5.19 by 11:30. Fast morning. Good morning.

The interesting part wasn't the button. It was realizing my mental model of my own cloud sync was wrong.

I've been telling myself for months it's timestamp-based last-write-wins. It's not. It's a lexicographic compare over monotonic counters — total IAPs first, then permanent boosts, then islands, ascensions, wealth, ads, clicks. Timestamp only breaks ties. Skins are union-merged and can never shrink. I *wrote* this. Past me designed something smarter than present me remembered, and present me has been confidently describing a system that doesn't exist. That guy was sharp. What happened to him.

There's a real gap in there too: individual permanent-boost ownership flags aren't union-merged, so a purchase made offline on one device could theoretically get eaten by another. Rare. I looked at it, weighed it, left it open. The physicist brain says the expected cost is tiny; the fix can wait for a day when Apple isn't tapping its foot. Writing it down here so it's a decision and not a thing I forgot.

Deployed to the phone. Launch failed because the phone was locked. The build pipeline, the sync semantics, the StoreKit choreography — all conquered. Defeated by a lock screen. Venga.

Now we wait on review. Again. Third round with 3.1.1. The button is good, the reasoning is sound, and none of that matters until a stranger in Cupertino agrees.
