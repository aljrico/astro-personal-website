Copy day. No code. Just words.

Sat down to rewrite the App Store description and discovered something mildly humiliating: my shiny new draft was worse than the one already live. Not entirely worse. Three ideas were genuinely good. But the bones of the shipped version were better, and I'd been about to bulldoze them because the new thing always feels smarter than the old thing. It isn't. Past me wrote decent copy. Present me showed up with a rewrite and mostly got told to sit down.

So it became a salvage job instead. Kept the good skeleton, grafted in the three keepers. The big one is the opener, the line people see before they tap "more": *Murder Sudoku is like Sudoku, but every puzzle is a crime scene.* That's the whole pitch in one sentence. If that line doesn't get someone, six paragraphs won't either. The second keeper was reframing the puzzle itself as "the moment of the murder", because a grid of numbers is boring but a frozen crime scene isn't.

The third keeper was the embarrassing one: the description didn't mention leaderboards. At all. I shipped competitive leaderboards, an actual feature people asked for, and the store page just... didn't know. The copy was frozen at some earlier version of the game while the game kept moving. That's the store-listing version of finding an old TODO comment for a thing you finished months ago.

While I was in there I checked the rest of the estate, in case the rot had spread. The British English locale turned out to never have had a tracked description at all, just promo text and release notes, so nothing to fix. And the Android listing is its own separate copy chasing its own search terms, and it *already mentioned leaderboards*. The Android page knew about my feature before the iOS one did. Same game, same me, two store listings drifting apart like estranged siblings. No idea what that says about my process. Probably nothing good.

Then the ritual purge: every em-dash, gone. They read like a machine wrote it, and this is the one page where strangers decide in four seconds whether I'm worth 80 megabytes of their phone. Commas and colons. Human punctuation. The Spanish and Catalan kept their casual register too, no opening ¡ or ¿, because nobody texts like the Real Academia.

Committed before noon, all three languages in one go. The words just sit in the repo now, waiting to ride the next release. Nobody will ever say "great description." But somewhere down the line, someone reads that opener, half-smiles, and taps Get. I'll never see it happen. That's the strange part of store copy: it's the most-read thing I'll write this month, and the only writing I do where the audience is real and I still get zero reaction either way.

Venga. Back to actual code tomorrow.
