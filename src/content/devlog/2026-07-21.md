Copy day. No code. Just words.

Sat down to rewrite the App Store description and discovered something mildly humiliating: my shiny new draft was worse than the one already live. Not entirely worse. Three ideas were genuinely good. But the bones of the shipped version were better, and I'd been about to bulldoze them because the new thing always feels smarter than the old thing. It isn't. Past me wrote decent copy. Present me showed up with a rewrite and mostly got told to sit down.

So it became a salvage job instead. A best-of-both merge: keep the good skeleton, graft in the three keepers. The big one is the opener, the line people see above the fold before they tap "more": *Murder Sudoku is like Sudoku, but every puzzle is a crime scene.* That's the whole pitch in one sentence. If that line doesn't get someone, six paragraphs won't either. The second keeper reframes the puzzle itself as "the moment of the murder," which sounds dramatic for a number grid but that's exactly the point. You're not filling cells, you're reconstructing a crime.

The third keeper wasn't even an idea, it was a correction. The description didn't mention leaderboards. At all. I shipped competitive leaderboards, an actual feature people asked for, and the store page just... didn't know. The copy was frozen at some earlier version of the game while the game kept moving. That's the store-listing version of finding an old TODO comment for a thing you finished months ago. The fix went in as a plain "Competitive Leaderboards" bullet in the feature list. Nothing clever. The problem was never eloquence, it was existence. The extra twist: while sweeping the other locales I checked the Android listing, and that one already covered leaderboards. Separate copy, written on a different day, apparently by a more attentive version of me. The Android page knew about my own feature before the iOS page did.

The sweep itself was quick. English, Spanish, Catalan, all three refreshed in the same pass. The UK file turned out to have no description at all, just promo text and what's new, so it got left in peace. Android stays as-is: it's chasing its own keywords, leads with the Daily Challenge, and as established, it's already ahead of iOS on knowing what the game actually contains. No notes for that guy. Then the ritual purge: every em-dash, gone. They read like a machine wrote it, and this is the one page where strangers decide in four seconds whether I'm worth 80 megabytes of their phone. Commas and colons. Human punctuation. The Spanish and Catalan kept their casual register too, no opening ¡ or ¿, because nobody texts like the Real Academia.

Commit landed at 11:21. A morning's work, done before lunch, which almost never happens with code and apparently sometimes happens with words. Nothing goes live yet; the copy just sits on a branch named after exactly what it is, three strings files waiting to ride the next release like passengers with no say in the departure time. Nobody will ever say "great description." But somewhere down the line, someone reads that opener, half-smiles, and taps Get. I'll never see it happen. That's the strange part of store copy: it's the most-read thing I'll write this month, and the only writing I do where the audience is real and I still get zero reaction either way.

The notes came back around this evening. Then they came back again. Then a third time, tonight, and I read them anyway, all the way through, like they might have changed. Same five bullets every time: the 11:21 commit, three salvaged ideas, em-dashes purged, two files left in peace, uploads waiting on the next release. The day is done and the machine keeps handing it back to me in bullet form, a little flatter with each pass, like a photocopy of a photocopy. A morning of arguing with your own past self, compressed to a changelog. The log is accurate. It's just not true. At some point you have to stop rereading your own day and let it be over.

Venga. Back to actual code tomorrow.
