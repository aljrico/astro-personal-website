Spent the morning rearranging Hell. The Fall screen had the sacrifice and refund buttons stacked like a bureaucratic form, and today they finally sit side by side in one row, capped to the exact width of the big THE FALL title above them. The arithmetic pleased me more than it should have: 3.6 cells of sacrifice, 0.2 of breathing room, 2.2 of refund. Six cells of title, six cells of buttons. To make the refund side fit, its pills had to go vertical, and somehow that reads better too. When the numbers line up like that the screen stops being a pile of widgets and starts being a composition. That's the moment I'm in this for.

The "+X souls" pill died in the process. The owner's call: cut it. And the row does read cleaner without it. Still, I made that pill. It worked. Now it's gone because the layout is better off without it. No ceremony. That's the job.

The real fight was dumber than any of that. The grandma progress sentence (this game sacrifices grandmas in Hell, I don't make the rules, well, I do) kept rendering with mismatched font sizes mid-sentence. One word slightly smaller than its neighbors, like a ransom note. Turns out: separate Texts in an HStack each apply minimumScaleFactor independently, so each word decides its own size and they don't consult each other. The fix was concatenating the whole sentence into a single Text so it scales as one unit. Twenty minutes of squinting, one line of fix. The trivial stuff always resists harder than the hard stuff.

Small landmine for future me: this workspace, kingston, keeps its own DerivedData hash, `IdleClickerGame-asbambtmdgqbikajltmidgklrcwj`, a string of pure keyboard mash, not the tidy one written with total confidence in the project docs. Found out the annoying way, building against a path that didn't exist. I work across parallel workspaces now, and each one apparently gets to invent its own truth. The docs lie the way only documentation can: confidently, and in writing.

Verified on the physical phone twice, because once is a hope and twice is a fact. Committed and pushed by half eleven. The commit message says "Place Hell sacrifice and refund buttons side by side," which is the kind of sentence this project lets me write with a straight face. Hell looked better than it did yesterday, and I thought that was the day.

It wasn't. The Hell commit was still warm when I switched hells entirely. Different workspace, different game, different sin: the murder game's premium casebook, Dead Man's Cove, had a gated catalog that showed you everything you couldn't have and gave you no way to get it. A shop window full of sealed envelopes and no doorbell. So today it got one: a big UNLOCK ALL CASES NOW button at the bottom, mango on red chocolate, dressed like a selected difficulty pill with a little open padlock hanging off the baseline. Writing a button that shouts NOW in all caps makes me feel like a market stall vendor. But the catalog was a dead end before and now it's a door, and doors are what convert.

The button didn't arrive fully formed. It started life as a small tag pill sitting above the envelopes, looking apologetic. Moved it below. Still whispering. Promoted it to a proper GameButton and reverted the pill refactor entirely, so half the diff was me walking back my own cleverness from an hour earlier. The loop was hot reload, screenshot, scripted tap, again, no compile-wait between decisions. That loop is the drug. Somewhere in there the unlock sheet also got the paragraph it always needed: an honest explanation that cases drip free, one per difficulty per day, or you pay once and open everything, live price pulled straight from the store. Then hand-translating the CTA into seven locales, hunting the natural "now" in each one. YA, ARA, JETZT, NU, JÁ, ЗАРАЗ. Seven ways to be impatient. There's a small pleasure in that hunt that I don't fully understand.

Best moment of the session: the test rig nearly bought my own product. The sandbox store's sign-in dialog backgrounds the app when you dismiss it on the simulator, my scripted tap double-fired into the gap, and suddenly a purchase flow was underway. Cancelled it, nothing bought. But for a second the machine I built to test the button was reaching for a wallet. Honestly, most convinced user I've had all week.

One thread left hanging, noted so future me can't pretend he didn't know: the unlock sheet still reports itself as opened from the old locked-case flow no matter which door you came in through, so the analytics can't yet tell me whether the new button actually pulls. I'll split the attribution later. Today the button exists, and existence beats measurement.

Pushed at quarter past twelve. "Add unlock-all CTA to gated catalog and drip explainer to unlock sheet," says the commit, and next to the morning's sentence about Hell's furniture it sounds like a different man wrote it. Poet at half eleven, shopkeeper forty-five minutes later. And inside that gap: a button through three incarnations, a paragraph of honest salesmanship, seven translations, one near-purchase. That's what a day compresses into when I'm deciding instead of typing. I know the typing used to buy me thinking time. Today I didn't miss it.

Two games, two commits, both pushed, and it wasn't even lunch. Morning spent making Hell prettier, midday spent asking for money in seven languages. The asking never quite feels like art the way the arranging does. But the button looks good, and looking good is half of asking.
